What Separates Good Prep from Great Prep

Every competitive debater prepares. They read the bills, jot down some arguments, maybe find a piece of evidence or two. That's good prep. But championship debaters do something fundamentally different — they prepare with intentionality, not just effort.

Great prep isn't more hours. It's more intentional hours. The debater who spends 45 focused minutes identifying the 2-3 strongest arguments per side, predicting what opponents will run, and building complete blocks will consistently outperform the debater who spends 3 hours casually browsing articles.

Here's what separates the two approaches. Good prep asks: "What can I say about this bill?" Great prep asks: "What are the 2-3 arguments that will actually win rounds on this bill, and what will my opponents say against them?"

Championship debaters don't just research both sides — they identify the strongest arguments per side AND predict what opponents will run. They prepare responses to those opposing arguments before they ever step into the round. This means when an opponent makes a point, the championship debater isn't surprised — they're ready.

The Intentionality Test
Before your next tournament, time yourself. If you spend more than 50% of your prep time reading and less than 50% writing arguments and blocks, your ratio is off. Reading is input. Writing is output. Championships are won on output.

The Prep Document

Championship debaters don't show up to tournaments with scattered notes on loose paper. They have a structured prep document — a single, organized resource that lets them find any argument, any evidence card, any refutation within seconds.

For each bill on the docket, your prep document should have four sections:

1. Affirmation Arguments — Your 2-3 strongest aff arguments, each with a complete SQUO-Outcome-Impact block. Lead with your best.

2. Negation Arguments — Your 2-3 strongest neg arguments, structured the same way. Even if you prefer one side, you need both ready.

3. Key Evidence Cards — The specific statistics, quotes, and citations that support your arguments. Each card should be tagged so you know which argument it supports.

4. Potential Refutations — For each of your arguments, note the most likely counter-argument and your response. This is your insurance policy when opponents challenge you.

The goal is simple: under the pressure of a live round, you should be able to find anything you need in under 10 seconds. If you're flipping through pages looking for a statistic while someone else is speaking, your prep document has failed you.

Depth over Breadth

One of the biggest mistakes debaters make is preparing 5-6 shallow arguments per side when they should be preparing 1-2 deep ones. This is consistent with the Block Format philosophy you've already learned — depth beats breadth every time.

A shallow argument sounds like: "This bill will help the economy." No specific mechanism, no evidence, no impact quantification. A judge has heard this a thousand times and it won't stand out.

A deep argument sounds like: "This bill's tax credit provision specifically targets manufacturing firms with under 500 employees, which according to the National Bureau of Economic Research accounts for 47% of new job creation. By reducing their effective tax rate by 3 points, the CBO projects 140,000 new jobs over 5 years — and that's conservative compared to what we saw after similar credits in 2015."

See the difference? The deep argument has a specific mechanism (tax credit for small manufacturers), specific evidence (NBER data, CBO projection), and a specific impact (140,000 jobs). It's nearly impossible to refute with a vague counter-argument.

Each argument in your prep should have a complete block: Status Quo problem, Outcome explanation, and Impact quantification — all backed by evidence cards. If you can't fill all three sections, the argument isn't ready yet.

The Depth Check
For each argument you've prepped, ask: "Could an opponent refute this with a single sentence?" If yes, it's not deep enough. Add specificity until a one-sentence refutation would sound absurd.

Lesson 4 of 5
Preparing for Adaptation

Here's an uncomfortable truth: your prep is a starting point, not a script. No matter how good your preparation is, the round will force you to adapt. Someone will cover your best argument. The debate will shift in a direction you didn't predict. A speaker before you will make a point that changes the landscape entirely.

Championship debaters prepare for this. They don't just have a Plan A — they have backup angles ready for when their primary argument gets covered. This means preparing at least one alternative approach per side that attacks the bill from a completely different angle.

For example, if your primary aff argument is economic (the bill creates jobs), your backup might be moral (the bill addresses a fundamental inequity). If your primary neg argument attacks the bill's mechanism (it won't work), your backup might attack the premise (the problem it claims to solve doesn't actually exist).

Adaptation also means knowing your material well enough to cut sections on the fly. If a previous speaker already established the Status Quo, you should be able to skip yours and jump straight to the Outcome without your speech falling apart. This requires understanding your argument deeply, not just memorizing a sequence of paragraphs.

Lesson 5 of 5
The Weekly Routine

Championship debaters don't cram the night before a tournament. They have a weekly rhythm that builds preparation gradually and consistently. Here's what it looks like:

Monday-Tuesday: Read the bills. Go through every bill on the docket. For each one, identify the core issue, note your initial reaction, and flag which ones you want to prioritize. Don't start researching yet — just understand the landscape.

Wednesday: Research. Deep dive into your priority bills. Find evidence, read expert analysis, look at both sides. This is your input day — consume information strategically.

Thursday: Write blocks. Turn your research into structured arguments. Build complete SQUO-Outcome-Impact blocks with evidence cards. This is your output day — transform information into ammunition.

Friday: Practice delivery. Read your blocks aloud. Time them. Identify which sections feel clunky and rewrite them. Practice adapting — try delivering your argument without the SQUO section, or with a different opening. Get comfortable with flexibility.

Saturday (Tournament Day): Trust your preparation. Review your prep document in the morning, but don't try to learn new material. You're executing, not cramming.

The key insight: consistency beats cramming every time. Four focused hours spread across a week produce better results than eight panicked hours the night before. Your brain needs time to process, connect, and internalize arguments.

The Compound Effect
If you follow this routine for 10 tournament cycles, you'll have 10 weeks of structured practice. The debater who crammed the night before each tournament has 10 isolated panic sessions. Over a season, the gap becomes enormous.
