What Separates Good Prep from Great Prep

Every competitive debater prepares. They read the bills, jot down some arguments, maybe find a piece of evidence or two. That's good prep. But championship debaters do something fundamentally different — they prepare with intentionality, not just effort.

Great prep isn't more hours. It's more intentional hours. The debater who spends 45 focused minutes identifying the 2-3 strongest arguments per side, predicting what opponents will run, and building complete blocks will consistently outperform the debater who spends 3 hours casually browsing articles.

Here's what separates the two approaches. Good prep asks: "What can I say about this bill?" Great prep asks: "What are the 2-3 arguments that will actually win rounds on this bill, and what will my opponents say against them?"

Championship debaters don't just research both sides — they identify the strongest arguments per side AND predict what opponents will run. They prepare responses to those opposing arguments before they ever step into the round. This means when an opponent makes a point, the championship debater isn't surprised — they're ready.

The Intentionality Test
Before your next tournament, time yourself. If you spend more than 50% of your prep time reading and less than 50% writing arguments and blocks, your ratio is off. Reading is input. Writing is output. Championships are won on output.

The Prep Document

Championship debaters don't show up with scattered notes. They have a structured prep document — a single resource that lets them find any argument, evidence card, or refutation in under 10 seconds.

For each bill, your prep document needs four sections:

✅
Aff Arguments
2-3 strongest aff arguments, each with a complete SQUO-Outcome-Impact block. Lead with your best.
❌
Neg Arguments
2-3 strongest neg arguments, structured the same way. Even if you prefer one side, you need both ready.
📊
Evidence Cards
Specific stats, quotes, and citations. Each tagged to the argument it supports so you can find it instantly.
🛡️
Refutations
For each argument, the most likely counter-argument and your response. Your insurance policy.

The 10-second rule: Under the pressure of a live round, you should be able to find anything you need in under 10 seconds. If you're flipping through pages while someone else is speaking, your prep document has failed you.

A good prep document only needs affirmation and negation arguments — evidence cards and refutations can be handled on the fly during the round.
Lesson 3 of 4
Depth over Breadth

One of the biggest mistakes debaters make is preparing 5-6 shallow arguments when they should be preparing 1-2 deep ones. See the difference:

❌ SHALLOW
"This bill will help the economy."
No mechanism. No evidence. No impact. Judges have heard this a thousand times.
✅ DEEP
"This bill's tax credit targets manufacturing firms under 500 employees — 47% of new job creation per NBER. The CBO projects 140,000 new jobs over 5 years."
Specific mechanism. Specific evidence. Specific impact. Nearly impossible to refute.

Each argument in your prep should have a complete block: SQUO problem, Outcome, and Impact — all backed by evidence. If you can't fill all three sections, the argument isn't ready yet.

The Depth Check
For each argument you've prepped, ask: "Could an opponent refute this with a single sentence?" If yes, it's not deep enough. Add specificity until a one-sentence refutation would sound absurd.

Lesson 4 of 4
Preparing for Adaptation

Here's an uncomfortable truth: your prep is a starting point, not a script. No matter how good your preparation is, the round will force you to adapt. Someone will cover your best argument. The debate will shift in a direction you didn't predict. A speaker before you will make a point that changes the landscape entirely.

Championship debaters prepare for this. They don't just have a Plan A — they have backup angles ready for when their primary argument gets covered. This means preparing at least one alternative approach per side that attacks the bill from a completely different angle.

For example, if your primary aff argument is economic (the bill creates jobs), your backup might be moral (the bill addresses a fundamental inequity). If your primary neg argument attacks the bill's mechanism (it won't work), your backup might attack the premise (the problem it claims to solve doesn't actually exist).

Adaptation also means knowing your material well enough to cut sections on the fly. If a previous speaker already established the Status Quo, you should be able to skip yours and jump straight to the Outcome without your speech falling apart. This requires understanding your argument deeply, not just memorizing a sequence of paragraphs.

If your primary argument gets covered by another speaker, the best strategy is to deliver it anyway since your version is likely stronger.
