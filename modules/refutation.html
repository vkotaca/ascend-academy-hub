Prerequisites & Refutation
You're not sponsoring the bill. The debate is already moving. How do you frame the round, engage with what's been said, and build your case at the same time? That's what prereqs and refutation are for.
Lesson 1 of 6
The Prerequisite Block

In other debate events like LD, PF, and Policy, speakers get an "off-time road map", they tell judges what they'll cover before their timer starts. Congress doesn't have that luxury. You get 10 seconds to walk up there and ask if the judges are ready.

That's why Block Format has the prerequisite block (or "prereq"). This is most applicable during later cycles when the debate is already in motion and you need to engage with what's been said. The prereq goes right after your intro and does two things:

  1. Summarize the clash: what has happened in the round so far between both sides
  2. Frame your argument: state your thesis or where you want to take the debate going forward

Think of it as a teaser for your case. You're giving judges a road map before diving into details. A prereq doesn't need to be long, half a block or less. It doesn't always need evidence cards. It just needs to orient the judges.

Lesson 2 of 6
Prereqs in Practice

There are two styles of prereq. Both work, choose based on what the round needs.

Style 1: Address the Opposition Directly
"So far, every single speaker on the negation has made the central claim that there isn't proof universal pre-K improves test scores. First, I think that ignores the fundamental data we've found. But beyond that, I don't think test scores are the only way to measure success. Let's look at other ways we can measure the value of universal pre-K."

Straightforward, you disagree with their framing and redirect the debate to your ground.
Style 2: Frame the Clash Between Both Sides
"So far in today's debate, affirmation senators have told you that pre-K will raise scores, while the negation has told you scores will be lowered. I personally don't think test scores are the only measure of whether pre-K is valuable. For many families, it's crucial they have a free option because beyond benefiting families, it benefits the development of these kids for years to come. Things that go beyond test scores."

Broader, you acknowledge both sides, then position yourself with a new lens on the debate.

The key in both cases: don't overthink it. A prereq is a quick summary of what's happened plus a statement of where you're heading. Treat it as a teaser, then deliver the goods in your blocks.

Lesson 3 of 6
Where Refutation Goes

If you built your block format correctly, your argument should naturally interact with the round. It should already be about the clash. But sometimes you need to directly address what the other side said. Here's where refutation can live:

Option 1: Beginning of a block. Start the block with the refutation, then let the rest of the block serve as your positive case. This sets up your block as a bigger response, "They said X, but here's why Y is true."

Option 2: End of a block. Give your block first (build your credibility with evidence and warrants), then say: "That's why when Senator Blank tells you this, it isn't true." You're establishing your logic first, then juxtaposing it with the opposition's weaker reasoning.

Option 3: Inside the prerequisite. If the refutation is about the larger round rather than your specific case, put it in the prereq. That's exactly what the prereq is for, addressing the round's clash before diving into your own argument.

Lesson 4 of 6
On-Case vs. Off-Case Refutation

Not all refutation is created equal. There are two types, and knowing the difference determines how you spend your time:

On-case refutation directly interacts with your argument. If your block is about the benefits of pre-K for child development, and the negation says pre-K doesn't help development; that's on-case. It naturally fits inside your blocks because your argument is already responding to it.

Off-case refutation addresses something the opposition said that isn't directly related to your specific argument, maybe it's a procedural objection or a tangential point. These should be relegated to after your main case or handled quickly at the top. They're not worth spending significant time on unless your main argument is fully delivered.

The Priority Rule
Always prioritize refutation that interacts with your case. Get everything necessary for your argument to make sense first. Off-case refutation only gets time when you've covered the essentials, don't sacrifice your own argument to refute a tangential point.

Lesson 5 of 6
Letting Go

This is one of the hardest skills in Congress: don't have attachment issues with your prep.

Sometimes your status quo has already been covered by a previous speaker. Sometimes your outcome block is similar to what someone else argued. When that happens, cut it. Let it go. It's not the end of the world.

Why? Because keeping information that's already been covered wastes time you could spend on what judges actually want to hear, fresh analysis, deeper refutation, or a stronger prereq. Judges reward speakers who add something new to the debate, not speakers who repeat what's already been said with slightly different evidence.

The Trade-Off
Dropping your status quo might feel painful, you spent time researching it. But that freed-up time could go toward in-round research for a stronger prereq card, or a deeper refutation that makes your speech feel like it lives in this round, not a pre-written template you're reading off a page.

Lesson 6 of 6
Know Your Debate

Every round develops differently, and the best debaters recognize what kind of debate they're in and adapt accordingly:

Status quo debate: Both sides are fighting over whether the current system is broken. Your refutation will naturally center on your status quo block. Spend more time there; that's where the clash is.

Outcome debate: Both sides agree there's a problem, but disagree on whether the legislation actually fixes it. Shift your refutation toward the outcome block. Cut down on status quo; it's not where the fight is.

The worst thing a Congress debater can do is be afraid to adapt. If the debate has moved past your prep, your speech will feel disconnected, like you're reading something you wrote at home instead of participating in the round happening in front of you. Flexibility is everything.

Putting It All Together
A complete non-sponsorship speech in block format: Intro → Prereq (frame the clash, tease your argument) → Blocks with integrated refutation (on-case refutation at the beginning or end of relevant blocks) → Off-case refutation (if time allows) → Conclusion. Stay flexible, cut what's redundant, and make every second count.
