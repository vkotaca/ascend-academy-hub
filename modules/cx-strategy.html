Cross-Examination Strategy
How to ask questions that expose weaknesses, handle hostile questioning, and use CX to set up your next speech.
Lesson 1 of 5
Why CX Matters

Cross-examination is one of the most underused scoring opportunities in Congressional Debate. Most debaters treat CX as a formality — a brief interruption between speeches. Championship debaters treat it as a weapon.

Here's why CX matters more than most competitors realize:

It's a scoring opportunity. Judges notice who asks questions and how sharp those questions are. A well-crafted CX question demonstrates intellectual engagement, critical thinking, and mastery of the issue. These are all things judges reward on the ballot.

It affects perception. Even if CX doesn't have a formal scoring category on every ballot, it shapes how judges view you. A debater who asks incisive questions is perceived as smarter, more prepared, and more engaged — and that perception carries over into how they evaluate your speech.

It sets up arguments. The most strategic use of CX is to preview your own argument. Ask about the weakness you're about to exploit. When the speaker gives a weak answer — and they usually will, because you've identified a genuine gap — you've just set up your next speech with a live demonstration of the flaw you're about to address.

You only get about 30 seconds. That's enough time for one, maybe two questions. Make them count.

Asking Good Questions

The best CX questions share two qualities: they are specific and they expose genuine gaps in the speaker's argument. Vague questions get vague answers. Specific questions force the speaker to either reveal a weakness or dodge the question — both of which work in your favor.

Effective question templates:

Challenge the evidence: "Your evidence is from 2016 — given that the policy landscape has shifted significantly since the 2020 reforms, do you have anything more current?" This targets outdated evidence with a specific reason why the date matters.

Expose a logical gap: "Your Status Quo describes the problem clearly, but your Outcome doesn't explain the specific mechanism by which this bill addresses it. Can you walk me through that causal chain?" This forces the speaker to either fill the gap (unlikely on the spot) or reveal that it exists.

Test the impact: "You mentioned this bill would create jobs, but you didn't quantify how many or over what timeframe. Can you be more specific?" This challenges vague impact claims with a demand for precision.

Reveal an unaddressed tradeoff: "You've argued this bill reduces costs, but haven't addressed the CBO's projection that it would also reduce coverage for 2 million people. How do you weigh those two outcomes?" This introduces information the speaker likely didn't prepare for.

Notice the pattern: every strong question is based on something specific the speaker said (or didn't say). You have to be listening carefully to ask good questions.

Lesson 3 of 5
Setting Up Your Speech

The most powerful use of CX is previewing the argument you're about to make. When done well, your CX question and your speech form a one-two punch that judges find incredibly compelling.

Here's how it works:

Step 1: Identify the weakness you plan to exploit. Before you ask your question, know exactly what gap in the speaker's argument you're going to build your speech around.

Step 2: Ask about that weakness. Your CX question should probe exactly the point where the speaker's argument is weakest. Their answer — whether a weak defense or an outright dodge — becomes exhibit A in your speech.

Step 3: Reference the CX exchange in your speech. When you stand up to speak, open with: "I asked the previous speaker about [X], and they couldn't provide a satisfactory answer. That's because [your argument]." The judge watched the exchange happen in real time — now you're framing it.

For example, if you're about to argue that a jobs bill lacks a credible implementation mechanism, ask the aff speaker: "Can you explain which specific agency would administer this program and with what funding?" When they fumble, you've just demonstrated live — in front of the judge — the exact weakness you're about to spend three minutes dissecting.

This technique is powerful because it turns CX from a standalone moment into the opening act of your speech.

Handling Hostile Questions

CX isn't just about asking questions — sometimes you're the one being questioned. And when a skilled questioner targets a genuine weakness in your argument, how you respond matters as much as what you say.

Four rules for handling tough CX questions:

1. Stay calm. The worst thing you can do is get flustered, defensive, or visibly rattled. Judges watch your composure under pressure. A calm response to a hard question earns more respect than a perfect response delivered with anxiety.

2. Be concise. Don't ramble. Long, winding answers look like you're trying to avoid the question. A brief, direct response — even if it doesn't fully address the point — is always better than a nervous monologue.

3. Pivot strategically. If a question targets a weakness you can't defend, redirect to your argument's strength: "That's a fair point about the implementation timeline, but the core of my argument is that the economic benefit of $47 billion outweighs those logistical concerns." You've acknowledged the point without conceding your entire argument.

4. Never get defensive. Phrases like "That's not what I said" or "You're twisting my argument" make you look combative. Instead, restate your position positively: "What I'm arguing is [X], and the evidence from [Y] supports that." Let the judge decide who's right.

The Composure Rule
If you don't know the answer to a CX question, don't fake it. Judges can tell. Instead, pivot: "I don't have that specific data point, but what I can tell you is that the broader trend supports my argument because..." Honesty with a redirect beats a bad bluff every time.

The 30-Second Rule

In Congressional Debate, CX periods are brief — typically 30 seconds. That's not enough time for a cross-examination battle. It's enough time for exactly one focused question.

This constraint means everything about your CX approach must be distilled to maximum efficiency:

Ask ONE question, not a compound. "How does your bill address both the funding gap AND the implementation timeline?" is two questions disguised as one. The speaker will answer whichever is easier and ignore the harder one. Instead, pick the more impactful question and ask it alone.

Make it specific. The more specific your question, the harder it is to dodge. "How does your bill fix this problem?" is easy to deflect. "Which provision in the bill creates the job training mechanism you described, and what's the projected cost per participant?" requires a real answer.

Don't ask questions you don't want answered. If the speaker has strong evidence on a point, don't ask about it. You'll give them a platform to reinforce their argument. Only ask questions where a weak answer (or no answer) helps you.

Know your question before the speech ends. Don't wait until CX starts to think of something. Formulate your question during the speech, so when the PO calls for questions, your hand goes up instantly. Speed signals preparation.

The Pre-Loaded Question
Write down your CX question while the speaker is still talking. By the time they finish, you should already have your question ready and your hand up. The first questioner gets the most attention from judges — and the best questions come from active listening, not last-second improvisation.
