Lesson 1 of 6
What Is an Impact?

You've proven a problem exists (SQUO). You've shown the bill fixes it (Outcome). Now comes the question every judge is silently asking: "So what? Why should I care?"

The Impact is your answer. It connects your argument to real human lives, not in the abstract, but in a way that makes judges feel the weight of the decision they're making. A good Outcome proves the bill works. A good Impact proves the bill matters.

Think of it this way: your SQUO and Outcome are the logical case. Your Impact is the emotional case. And in Congressional Debate, where judges are ranking speeches, not just arguments, the debater who makes judges feel something gets ranked higher than the debater who merely informs them.

An Impact is not just "this is good for the economy" or "this helps people." It answers specifically: HOW do people's lives change? What does their Tuesday morning look like in the world where this bill passes versus the world where it doesn't?

Humanizing Your Impact

Statistics prove your argument is real. But the human connection is what makes judges remember it when they fill out their ballot. Data informs. Stories persuade.

"13 million workers hold two jobs" is a strong statistic. But watch what happens when you humanize it: "Picture a single mother who drops her kids off at school, works an 8-hour shift at a grocery store, picks up her kids, feeds them dinner, puts them to bed, and then drives to her overnight shift at a warehouse. She does this five days a week, and she still can't afford rent."

Judges remember that mother. They don't remember "13 million." The statistic gives you credibility. The human story gives you the ballot.

The best impacts use both: lead with the human story, then anchor it with data. "That single mother isn't alone, 13 million Americans are living this reality." Now judges feel it AND believe it.

One important rule: your humanized impact must stem from your proven argument. You can't suddenly introduce a new storyline in the Impact that your SQUO and Outcome didn't set up. The human story must be a direct illustration of the problem and solution you already proved.

Abstract Impact (informative but forgettable)
"Raising the minimum wage would improve the financial conditions of low-income Americans and contribute to better economic outcomes."
Humanized Impact (persuasive and memorable)
"When low-income Americans no longer have to make the choice between being overworked and exhausted or underpaid and malnourished, it can be life-saving. A single mother working two jobs can finally be home for dinner. A father who hasn't had a day off in three years can take his kid to a baseball game."

Lesson 3 of 6
Multiple Impacts from One Argument

A common question: "Can I have more than one impact?" The answer is yes, as long as every impact stems from the same core argument. You're not making new arguments in the Impact section. You're showing the full downstream effect of the one argument you already proved.

Think of it like dropping a stone in a pond. Your argument is the stone. The impacts are the ripples. Each ripple goes further out, but they all come from the same point of origin.

Here's how this works with the minimum wage overworking argument:

💰
Economic Impact
Workers earning a livable wage spend more in their local economies, creating a multiplier effect that boosts small businesses.
🩹
Health Impact
Workers who aren't overworked have lower rates of depression, heart disease, and premature death.
👪
Quality of Life Impact
Parents who work one job instead of two can be present for their children, reducing the cycle of poverty and neglect.

All three impacts flow from the same argument: higher wages reduce overworking. The economic, health, and quality-of-life impacts are downstream consequences, ripples from the same stone. This is perfectly valid and makes your argument sound more comprehensive.

What you cannot do is introduce an impact that requires a completely different argument. If your block is about overworking, you can't suddenly impact on "this bill also reduces the trade deficit" — that's a separate argument needing its own SQUO and Outcome.

Lesson 4 of 6
The Comparative

Judges ultimately make one decision: is the world better with this bill or without it? The comparative is the rhetorical tool that forces them to make that choice explicitly.

A comparative frames your impact as a direct comparison between two worlds. Instead of just describing what happens if the bill passes, you show both possibilities side by side, making the right choice feel obvious.

The standard framework sounds like this: "In a world where we pass this bill, X happens. In a world where we don't, Y continues." This structure works because it mirrors the actual decision judges are making. They're not deciding if the bill is perfect; they're deciding if the bill's world is better than the status quo's world.

The comparative is especially powerful late in a round when multiple arguments are on the table. It cuts through the noise: "You've heard a lot of arguments today. But here's the fundamental choice: in one world, 13 million workers come home for dinner. In the other, they keep working two jobs until their health gives out. Which world do you want to vote for?"

Impact Without Comparative
"This bill will help workers live healthier lives and spend more time with their families."
Impact With Comparative
"In a world where we pass this bill, a mother working two jobs can finally drop her overnight shift and be home when her kids wake up. In a world where we don't, she keeps choosing between rent and rest until her health collapses. That's the choice before this chamber."

Lesson 5 of 6
Terminalization

Every impact chain ends somewhere. Terminalization is the practice of tracing your impact to its most fundamental endpoint, the deepest value at stake. Some impacts terminalize stronger than others:

🔴
Tier 1: Lives & Safety
"This saves lives" or "This prevents deaths" — the strongest terminal impact. If you can credibly trace your argument here, do it.
🟠
Tier 2: Rights & Dignity
"This restores fundamental human dignity" or "This protects constitutional rights" — nearly as powerful. Appeals to principles judges deeply hold.
🟡
Tier 3: Economic Stability
"This prevents families from falling into poverty" — strong when connected to real families, weaker when it stays abstract.
⚪
Tier 4: Efficiency & Systems
"This makes government more efficient" — the weakest. Abstract, impersonal, and doesn't connect to human experience.

The key insight: "This saves lives" is always stronger than "This improves efficiency." If you can trace your impact chain to lives, safety, or fundamental rights, do it. Don't stop at the economic level if you can go deeper.

Lesson 6 of 6
Impact in Practice

Let's build a complete Impact section from scratch. We'll use a criminal justice reform bill that eliminates mandatory minimum sentences for nonviolent drug offenses.

Our argument throughout the block has been: mandatory minimums force nonviolent offenders into years of incarceration, destroying their families and communities, and the bill's elimination of these minimums allows judges to sentence proportionally.

Here's how we construct the Impact step by step:

1
Start with the Human Story
"Imagine a 22-year-old caught with a small amount of marijuana. Under current law, a judge who believes probation is appropriate is forced by mandatory minimums to sentence them to five years in federal prison. That person loses their job, their apartment, their relationship. Their child grows up visiting a parent behind glass."
2
Anchor with Data
"That story isn't hypothetical. The Bureau of Justice Statistics reports that 46% of federal inmates are serving time for drug offenses, and the average mandatory minimum sentence is 5 to 10 years. 2.7 million children in America have an incarcerated parent."
3
Use the Comparative
"In a world where we pass this bill, judges regain the ability to sentence proportionally. That 22-year-old gets probation and community service, they keep their job, they raise their child, they contribute to society. In a world where we don't, they join the 46% of federal inmates serving years for nonviolent offenses while their family falls apart."
4
Terminalize
"When we talk about mandatory minimums, we're not talking about policy — we're talking about whether 2.7 million children get to grow up with a parent. We're talking about whether justice means proportional accountability or automatic destruction of families. If you believe that a punishment should fit the crime, make sure you say it with your vote."

Notice the structure: human story → data anchor → comparative → terminalization. The Impact doesn't introduce a new argument; it takes the proven argument (mandatory minimums destroy lives) and makes judges feel the weight of it. By the time you reach the terminalization, the choice feels obvious.
