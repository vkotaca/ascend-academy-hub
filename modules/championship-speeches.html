Lesson 1 of 6
Early Round. Tyler Luu on Redistricting

The first speech in this video is Tyler Luu affirming a redistricting bill. This is a textbook first affirmative (sponsorship) speech, watch how cleanly it follows Block Format fundamentals.

Pro Tip
Tyler Luu competes at the national level. Pay attention to how his early-round speech is structured to be clear and self-contained, this is the standard you should aim for in sponsorship speeches.
What to Watch For
Status Quo with clean data: Tyler opens with a powerful statistic, 84% of Americans distrust how districts are drawn. One number that proves the problem exists and is specific to HIS argument (trust in redistricting), not the broad topic of gerrymandering.

Past Precedent Outcome: He uses Michigan, Colorado, and California, states that enacted the exact same system, to show candidates were 225% more likely to face competitive elections. Past precedent is one of the strongest evidence types because it proves the mechanism WORKS in the real world.

Terminalized Impact: Restored competition leads to increased voter turnout, which leads to real policy change (minimum wage, welfare, child support). Notice the chain: competition → trust → turnout → policy that affects lives.

Intro/Conclusion Theme Tie: Tyler opens with a theme about belief and democracy, then returns to it in his conclusion. The speech feels like one complete story, not a list of disconnected points.

Lesson 2 of 6
Early Round. Anish Beeram on Police Training Standards

This speech on police training standards is a masterclass in prereqs and integrated on-case refutation. Anish Beeram enters a debate that's already in progress, he needs to address the negation's arguments directly before building his own case.

Pro Tip
Watch how Anish addresses the negation's police shortage argument directly with evidence, then pivots to quality of policing. This is how you weave refutation into your own case without losing your structure.
What to Watch For
Prereq as refutation: Anish opens by directly addressing the negation's police shortage argument with hard data: 87% of police funding comes from local governments, not Congress. He doesn't just disagree, he proves the neg's argument is factually misplaced, then dismisses it as "tried, tired, and trivial." That's refutation with teeth.

SQUO pivot (quantity → quality): After dismantling the neg, Anish pivots to his own case: the bill improves the QUALITY of policing. He reframes the entire debate from "will we have enough officers?" to "will officers be properly trained?"

Evidence depth: 15% of officers have no education beyond high school → that 15% commits 75% of disciplinary violations. Two cards that build on each other to make the problem undeniable.

Impact with data: Officers with degrees are 40% less likely to use force, 30% less likely to use a weapon. This is impact WITH evidence, the Block Format difference from CWEI.

Personal rhetoric: "I am tired of turning on the TV and hearing another story of an innocent American being shot in the streets." Personal, emotional, authentic, not performed. This lands because three minutes of substance earned the right to get emotional.

Lesson 3 of 6
Early Round. Tyler Luu on Qualified Immunity

Tyler Luu returns with a speech on qualified immunity, a complex legal topic. Watch how he uses Block Format with a strong evidence chain to make this accessible to judges who may not know the legal details.

Pro Tip
Tyler Luu delivered this speech during 2023 NSDA Senate Finals. Notice how he narrows the topic, he doesn't try to explain all of qualified immunity. He focuses on one specific mechanism (de-escalation training) and proves that mechanism works.
What to Watch For
Making complexity accessible: Qualified immunity is a dense legal topic. Tyler doesn't try to cover all of it, he picks one angle (lack of de-escalation training) and builds his entire block around that specific mechanism. This is how you win on complex topics: narrow, then go deep.

SQUO focused on training, not QI broadly: His status quo doesn't broadly attack qualified immunity as a legal concept; it focuses specifically on the lack of de-escalation training. This is strategic: by narrowing his SQUO, his Action and Outcome become provable.

Outcome that proves the mechanism: Tyler provides evidence that when the specific change in the bill is implemented, it produces measurable results. The outcome section exists to prove the link between Action and Impact.

Dual impacts, officers AND communities: His impact chain protects BOTH officers and the communities they serve. This is powerful because it preempts the neg's likely argument that reform hurts police. By showing the bill helps both sides, Tyler removes the neg's strongest ground.
Multiple Choice
Why does Tyler Luu focus his SQUO on de-escalation training rather than qualified immunity as a whole?
Lesson 4 of 6
Mid Round. Adrian Baek on Medicare for All

This speech comes later in the round when the debate is well-developed. Adrian Baek enters a developed debate, watch how he doesn't introduce a brand-new argument from scratch but instead crystallizes the debate by engaging with what both sides have said and delivering the decisive comparison.

Pro Tip
Adrian Baek demonstrates what late-round crystallization looks like. He addresses multiple speakers by name, flips the neg's own evidence, and still delivers a complete case. That combination is the skill that separates good debaters from great ones.
What to Watch For
Prereq addressing multiple speakers by name: "Senator Mishra and Dupal tell you about costs while Senator Lung tells you about wait times." Adrian acknowledges the neg's case, then systematically dismantles each point. This shows judges he's been listening to the ENTIRE round.

Flipping neg's own evidence: The neg argued EU data proves we need private healthcare. Adrian flips it: outside of Slovakia, the EU only allows private coverage to be provided by public entities. The neg's own evidence supports his side.

Commonwealth Fund comparative across 4 metrics: Adrian uses the Commonwealth Fund to directly compare the US (with private sector) vs. European nations (with universal coverage) across four metrics. The US performed worse on all four. This is the two-world comparison in action.

Personal intro/conclusion tie: Adrian opens with a personal story about his mother, then closes by returning to that theme, connecting personal experience to legislative urgency. The speech is one unified narrative.

Lesson 5 of 6
Mid Round. Aditya Chordiya on CARSI / Foreign Aid

Aditya Chordiya delivers a speech on CARSI (Central America Regional Security Initiative) that is a masterclass in playing offense by turning the negation's strongest argument. Instead of just defending his side, he takes the neg's best point and makes it a reason to affirm.

Pro Tip
Aditya's offensive flip is one of the cleanest in this video. The neg argues gangs extort foreign aid NGOs, and Aditya turns that into a reason to affirm. This is the difference between defense ("that's not true") and offense ("that actually helps my side").
What to Watch For
The offensive flip: The neg argues gangs extort foreign aid NGOs. Aditya's response: the existence of affirmation evidence means that any amount of corruption or extortion has not been enough to decrease the efficacy of the programs. If the programs work despite extortion, the neg's argument is irrelevant.

Quantifying solvency: 346,000 students prevented from joining gangs. Gang membership decreased by 39% in CARSI areas. Aditya doesn't just assert the program works, he proves it with specific numbers that are impossible to hand-wave away.

The logical trap: Gangs would have tried to extort something that actively hurts their recruitment. Aditya walks the judges through the logic step by step until the neg's position becomes absurd.

Impact chain: Decreased gang recruitment → decreased gang violence → decreased police-gang shootings → fewer atrocities. Each link follows from the last. This is Block Format's link chain at its best.

Closing rhetoric: "Help. Hope. Humanity." Three words, terminalized to values, tied to the legislation. Clean and memorable.

Lesson 6 of 6
Late Round. Combined Analysis

This final lesson examines two late-round speeches that demonstrate the highest-level skills in Congressional Debate: Rohit Jhawar on personal data privacy and Aditya Chordiya crystallizing the police training debate.

Rohit Jhawar. Data Privacy (Aff)
Gap-filling mindset: Rohit explicitly identifies what previous affirmation speakers failed to do: "No one in this round has quantified how often they sell your data or actually even proved that it happens." He tells judges exactly why his speech matters, he's delivering what they haven't heard yet.

Correcting his own side: Rohit doesn't just fill gaps, he corrects his own side's assumptions. Previous speakers assumed Americans would be fine with data collection if informed. He calls that out as false. That takes strategic courage.

Flipping neg economics: The neg says the bill hurts ad revenue. Rohit cites Harvard Business Review: targeted ads DECREASE product interest by 24%. The neg's own economic argument backfires; that's offense, not defense.
Aditya Chordiya. Police Training Standards (Aff, Crystallization)
Moral framing: "I morally oppose the shortage argument." Aditya doesn't just logically refute, he takes a moral stance. This is rhetoric at its highest level: making judges feel that voting neg is morally untenable.

Addressing opponents by name: Aditya calls out specific opponents and their arguments, showing judges he's been listening to the entire round and directly engaging with what was said.

New DOJ evidence even in the final speech: DOJ issued $1.2 billion in grants for de-escalation and community policing. Even in a crystallization speech, Aditya introduces fresh data that advances the aff case. You don't stop building just because it's the end.

Flipping neg concern (officer safety): De-escalation training reduced officer injuries by 11-36% AND community use-of-force by 23-40%. Aditya takes the neg's concern and shows the aff SOLVES it better. That's offense.

Emotional close: Aditya closes by challenging opponents who invoke names like George Floyd while defending the status quo. This is the kind of line judges remember when filling out the ballot, pointed, personal, and earned by three minutes of substance.
