Lesson 1 of 6
Redistricting — Block Format Fundamentals

The first speech in this video is Tyler Luu affirming a redistricting bill. Watch how cleanly it follows Block Format — this is a textbook first affirmative (sponsorship) speech.

What to Watch For
Intro: Opens with humor about friendship — immediately memorable, establishes personality before substance. Notice how the intro THEME (partisanship/trust) connects directly to his argument.

Status Quo: "84% of Americans think districts are unfair and uncompetitive." One clean statistic that proves the problem. Notice it's specific to HIS argument (trust in redistricting), not the broad topic of gerrymandering.

Action → Outcome: References the legislation, then uses past precedent from Michigan, Colorado, and California — states that enacted the exact same system. Candidates were 225% more likely to have competitive elections. Past precedent is powerful because it proves the mechanism WORKS.

Impact: Restored democracy → increased voter turnout → policy changes (minimum wage, welfare, child support). Notice how he terminalizes: competition → trust → turnout → real policy that affects lives.

Conclusion: Ties back to the intro theme of belief and democracy. The speech feels like one complete story.

Lesson 2 of 6
Police Reform — Prereqs & On-Case Refutation

This speech on police reform and education requirements is a masterclass in integrated refutation. The speaker isn't giving the first speech — they're entering a debate that's already in progress, which means they need a prereq and refutation woven into their blocks.

What to Watch For
Prereq as refutation: Opens by directly addressing the negation's police shortage argument: "Just because today's legislation does not talk about pay does not mean pay will not be raised." He doesn't just disagree — he provides a warrant (87% of police funding comes from local governments, not Congress) and calls the argument "tried, tired, and trivial." That's refutation with teeth.

SQUO pivot: After dismantling the neg, he pivots to his own case: "I'd contend you are passing this bill to improve the QUALITY of policing." He reframes the debate from quantity to quality.

Evidence depth: 15% of officers have no education beyond high school → that 15% commits 75% of disciplinary violations. Two cards that build on each other to make the problem undeniable.

Impact with evidence: Officers with degrees are 40% less likely to use force, 30% less likely to use a weapon. This is impact WITH data — the Block Format difference from CWEI.

Rhetoric: "I am tired of turning on the TV and hearing another story of an innocent American being shot in the streets." Personal, emotional, authentic — not performed.

Lesson 3 of 6
Medicare for All — Late-Round Crystallization

This speech comes later in the round when the debate is well-developed. Watch how the speaker doesn't introduce a brand new argument — instead, he crystallizes the debate by engaging with what both sides have said and delivering the decisive comparison.

What to Watch For
Prereq that addresses multiple speakers by name: "Senator Mishra and Dupal tell you about costs while Senator Lung tells you about wait times." He acknowledges the neg's case, then systematically dismantles each point. This shows judges he's been listening to the ENTIRE round.

Reframing: The neg argued Europe proves we need private healthcare. He flips it: "Outside of Slovakia, the EU only allows private coverage to be provided by public entities." The neg's own evidence supports his side.

The comparative: Uses the Commonwealth Fund to directly compare the US (with private sector) vs. European nations (with universal coverage) across four metrics. The US performed worse on all four. This is the two-world comparison in action.

Intro/conclusion tie: Opens with a personal story about his mother, closes by returning to that theme — "today let it drive this nation forward." The speech is one unified narrative.

The lesson: Late-round speeches win by engaging with the round, not ignoring it. He addresses 4+ speakers by name and still delivers his own case. That's the skill.

Lesson 4 of 6
Data Privacy — Filling Gaps

This speech on data privacy demonstrates the gap mindset in action. The speaker explicitly identifies what previous affirmation speakers failed to do — then fills that gap himself.

What to Watch For
Identifying the gap: "No one in this round has quantified how often they sell your data or actually even proved that it happens. Let's pick up where the rest of the affirmation dropped the ball." He's telling judges exactly why his speech matters — he's delivering what they haven't heard yet.

Quantifying what others couldn't: 70% of smartphone apps sell your data, $21 billion industry, 200 million Americans affected. Previous speakers asserted the problem; he PROVES it with specific numbers.

Correcting his own side: "Everyone else in this round has just assumed that if you tell Americans their data is getting collected they'll be fine with it. That's categorically false." He's not just filling gaps in the debate — he's correcting his own side's assumptions. That takes strategic courage.

Flipping the neg's economics: The neg says the bill hurts ad revenue. He cites Harvard Business Review: targeted ads DECREASE product interest by 24%. The neg's own economic argument backfires. This is offense, not defense.

CX handling: Watch his CX responses — concise, direct, never flustered. When pressed on whether companies would accept the change, he pivots: "Companies that don't sell data are having more effective ads."

Lesson 5 of 6
Foreign Aid (CARSI) — Playing Offense

This speech on CARSI (Central America Regional Security Initiative) is a masterclass in offense over defense. Instead of just defending his side, the speaker takes the negation's strongest argument and turns it into a reason to affirm.

What to Watch For
The offensive flip: The neg argues gangs extort foreign aid NGOs. His response: "The existence of affirmation evidence means that any amount of corruption or extortion has not been enough to decrease the efficacy of our programs." If the programs work despite extortion, the neg's argument is irrelevant.

Quantifying solvency: 346,000 students prevented from joining gangs. Gang membership decreased by 39% in CARSI areas. He doesn't just assert the program works — he proves it with specific numbers that are impossible to hand-wave away.

The logical trap: "Gangs would have tried to extort something that actively hurts their recruitment. Oh wait — they literally couldn't." He walks the judges through the logic step by step until the neg's position becomes absurd.

Broader impact chain: Decreased gang recruitment → decreased gang violence → decreased police-gang shootings → fewer atrocities. Each link follows from the last. This is Block Format's link chain at its best.

Closing rhetoric: "Help. Hope. Humanity. If you believe in those principles, help reunite the Northern Triangle." Three words, terminalized to values, tied to the legislation. Clean.

Lesson 6 of 6
Final Crystallization — Tying It All Together

The final speech in the police reform debate is a crystallization — the speaker's job is no longer to introduce new arguments but to tell judges why the affirmation wins the debate. Watch how every technique from this curriculum comes together in one speech.

What to Watch For
Moral framing: "I morally oppose the shortage argument." He doesn't just logically refute — he takes a moral stance. This is rhetoric at its highest level: making judges feel that voting neg is morally untenable.

Addressing opponents by name: "Representative Benoi, you then say police training is bad but then you advocate for the same status quo which kills Americans." Calling out specific opponents shows judges you've been listening and you're directly engaging with the round.

New evidence in the final speech: DOJ issued $1.2 billion in grants for deescalation and community policing. Even in a crystallization speech, he introduces fresh data that advances his case. You don't stop building just because it's the end.

Flipping neg evidence: Deescalation training reduced officer injuries by 11-36% AND community use-of-force by 23-40%. He takes the neg's concern (officer safety) and shows the aff SOLVES it better. That's offense.

The emotional close: "Representatives Dixon and Andrew, please do not use the names of George Floyd when you were advocating for the same status quo that got him killed." This is the kind of line judges remember when filling out the ballot. It's pointed, it's personal, and it's earned by three minutes of substance.
