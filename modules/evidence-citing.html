What makes evidence credible, how to cite it in round, and how to attack weak evidence from opponents.
Lesson 1 of 4
What Makes Evidence Credible

Not all evidence is created equal. In Congressional Debate, judges evaluate the quality of your sources just as much as the content of your arguments. Using weak evidence can actually hurt you more than using no evidence at all — because it signals that you don't know the difference.

Always ask yourself: "Would an actual member of Congress use this source?" Here's the hierarchy:

High Quality:

  • Think tanks — Brookings, Carnegie Endowment, Peterson Institute
  • Academic institutions & journals — Harvard, Stanford + American Economic Review, Journal of Public Economics
  • Congressional research agencies — CRS and CBO (nonpartisan, designed for Congress)
  • Select news publications only — NYT, WSJ, Washington Post, LA Times, The Economist, Bloomberg

Low Quality (avoid):

  • Most news outlets — ABC, AP, CBS, CNN, Fox, HuffPost, NBC, Newsweek, Reuters, Time
  • Publicly created sources — Wikipedia, social media, Reddit
  • Opinion pieces & advocacy organizations — clear agendas, judges discount heavily

Beyond source quality, two other factors matter: recency (2023 data beats 2015 data — conditions change) and specificity (exact numbers beat vague claims like "studies show").

The Credibility Shortcut
When in doubt, start your research with the Congressional Budget Office (CBO) and Congressional Research Service (CRS). Both are nonpartisan, highly respected, and specifically analyze legislation — exactly what you need in Congress.

How to Cite in Round

Citing evidence in a speech isn't the same as writing an academic paper. You need to be brief, clear, and authoritative. Judges need enough information to trust your source, but not so much that your citation eats into your argument time.

The standard Congressional Debate citation format is: "Author or Organization, Publication, Date: [quote or paraphrase]."

For example: "According to Ben Zipperer at the Economic Policy Institute, February 2019, a $15 minimum wage would raise earnings for 33.5 million workers while causing minimal job displacement."

Key rules for in-round citations:

1. Lead with the organization. "The Congressional Budget Office" carries more weight than a name judges don't recognize. If the author is well-known, use their name. If not, lead with the organization.

2. Include the year. Judges will mentally discount evidence that sounds old. If your source is recent, make sure they know: "...in their 2024 analysis..."

3. Keep it flowing. Weave citations into your speech naturally: "The CBO's March 2024 analysis confirms this — they project a 2.1% GDP increase over five years." Don't stop your speech to formally announce a citation.

4. Never cite the URL. Judges don't need "www.cbo.gov/publication/59234." They need the organization, date, and the relevant finding.

Lesson 3 of 4
Using Evidence Strategically

Evidence is a tool, not a crutch. The most common mistake debaters make is treating evidence as a substitute for reasoning: they cite a statistic and act like the argument is made. Evidence supports your reasoning — it doesn't replace it.

Here's the strategic approach:

One strong card is better than three weak ones. If you have one study from the CBO and three from opinion blogs, use only the CBO study. Stacking weak evidence dilutes your credibility — judges notice when you're padding.

Lead with your best evidence. Place your most powerful statistic or study early in your argument. First impressions matter, and judges are most attentive at the start of your speech. Don't save your best card for the end when attention has waned.

Place statistics where they reinforce your warrant. A warrant is your reasoning — the "why" behind your argument. Statistics should land right after your warrant to prove it: "This bill's job training provision works because it targets industries with documented labor shortages. The Bureau of Labor Statistics reports 1.8 million unfilled positions in skilled trades as of 2024 — exactly the gap this program is designed to close."

Never let evidence stand alone. Always explain what your evidence means for the debate. "The CBO says X" isn't enough. "The CBO says X, which means Y for this bill, because Z" — that's a complete evidentiary argument.

Attacking Weak Evidence

Knowing how to evaluate evidence isn't just about strengthening your own speeches — it's about identifying and exposing weaknesses in opponents' evidence during CX or in your own speeches. This is one of the most effective ways to undermine an opposing argument.

Four common evidence weaknesses to target:

1. Outdated sources. Data from 2014 about the economy, healthcare, or technology is practically ancient. Challenge it directly: "Senator, your evidence is from 2016. Given that the policy landscape has shifted dramatically since then, do you have anything more current?"

2. Biased organizations. If an opponent cites an advocacy group, point out the bias: "Your source, the National Association of [Industry], has a financial interest in opposing this regulation. Do you have a nonpartisan source that supports the same conclusion?"

3. Cherry-picked statistics. Opponents often cite one favorable data point while ignoring the broader trend. Call it out: "You cited the one quarter where employment dropped, but the overall trend shows 18 months of consecutive growth. One data point doesn't override the trend."

4. Evidence that doesn't prove the claim. Sometimes evidence sounds relevant but doesn't actually support the argument being made. "Your study shows that people support this policy in polls, but public opinion doesn't prove the policy will be effective. Those are two different questions."

The CX Evidence Trap
In CX, ask opponents about the date and source of their evidence. Many debaters cite vague "studies" without knowing the actual source. A simple "Can you tell me which study that's from?" often reveals that they don't know — and judges notice.
