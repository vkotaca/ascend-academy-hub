Why Argument Formats Matter

In competitive debate, how you structure your argument is just as important as what you argue. A great argument badly organized loses to a mediocre argument clearly presented.

Formats give you three things: efficiency in prep (you always know exactly what to write), a shared language between debater and judge (so judges can follow and score your argument), and depth — the ability to explore a single idea fully instead of skimming across three shallow ones.

Most debaters are taught the CWEI structure: Claim, Warrant, Evidence, Impact — and then told to make 2-3 arguments in one speech. Block Format flips this: one argument, explored deeply. That's the entire philosophy.

The Origin of Block Format

In 2019, Quest Sandel was still teaching the CWEI 2-3 argument style — but felt limited. He and Rohit Jhawar wanted something that would allow a debater to thoroughly prove a single idea rather than gesture at three weak ones.

Quest systematized the one-argument approach into what we now call Block Format. Rohit Jhawar won the NSDA Championship using it that same year.

Over six years of use and refinement, Block Format users have won 5 TOCs, the majority of major invitationals, and state championships across Congress-oriented states.

The Core Insight
"It is easier for judges to understand a single link chain of A → B → C instead of three of them. When you try to run three arguments, you give each argument less time, less depth, and less persuasive force. Judges remember one powerful idea far longer than three weak ones."

The 3 Components of Block Format

Every block — regardless of bill or side — has exactly three main components. Think of them as three acts in a story your judges need to follow.

🔭
Status Quo
What's the current situation? Establish the problem that makes this legislation necessary.
⚡
Outcome
What does the bill do? And what new world does that action create?
💥
Impact
Why should judges care? Humanize and connect to real lives.

The three components are designed to form a single, unbroken link chain: the Status Quo proves there's a problem, the Outcome proves the bill solves it, the Impact proves why that solution matters.

Lesson 4 of 8
The Status Quo

The Status Quo answers one question: "What is the current state of the world as it relates to your argument?" This isn't about the bill's topic broadly — it's the specific slice of reality that your argument is trying to change.

A strong SQUO does two things simultaneously: it provides judges with the necessary context to understand your argument, and it establishes that a problem exists — so that when you present your Outcome, judges already feel the urgency.

Crucially: the SQUO is unique to your argument, not just the topic. Two debaters can argue the same bill with completely different SQUOs because they're proving different things. And a great SQUO can also function as preemptive refutation — if you prove the problem clearly, opponents have to prove you're wrong about the problem before they can win the Outcome battle.

Example SQUO (Minimum Wage Bill)
"Workers, who are the backbones of companies, are being squeezed for profit. Ben Zipperer, EPI, Feb 7 2019: workers paid the federal minimum wage of $7.25 are, after adjusting for inflation, paid 29% less than their counterparts 50 years ago — despite the economy's capacity to deliver higher wages having doubled."

The Outcome (& Action)

The Outcome is your argument — it's where you prove what this legislation actually does to change the world. It has two parts bundled together:

The Action is a 1-2 sentence factual summary of what the bill does, referencing the specific sections. "Section 1 of this legislation does... and Section 2 does..." This is not arguable — it's purely context. Judges need to understand the mechanism before they can evaluate the outcome.

The Outcome itself is the argument: what does that action create? Congress ultimately comes down to a battle of outcomes — which world (the bill's world vs. the status quo world) is better for Americans?

Example Action + Outcome (Minimum Wage)
"Pass this bill to decrease overworking. Sec. 1 raises the minimum wage to $15/hr. The Census Bureau, June 2019: more than 13 million minimum wage workers have to work more than one permanent job just to afford rent. A Walden University study found those working 11+ hours daily are 2.5x more likely to develop depression. 13 million Americans are risking their health working two jobs — but when you double their wages, most workers will only need one job."

The Impact

The Impact answers the final question judges always have: "So what? Why should I care?" It's your job to make the stakes real, human, and emotionally resonant — not just statistically impressive.

Three keys to a great Impact:

1. Humanize it. Connect your argument down to life, health, safety, or quality of life. Abstract policy outcomes don't win rounds — real people do.

2. Connect to your judges. Show how the average American is affected. If your judges can see themselves or their families in your impact, they'll remember it when they write their ballot.

3. Multiple impacts are fine — as long as they all stem from the same argument. You're not making a new argument; you're showing the full downstream consequences of the one you've already proven.

Example Impact (Minimum Wage)
"Ultimately, when low-income Americans no longer have to make the choice between being overworked and exhausted or underpaid and malnourished, it can be life saving. The American Journal of Public Health, March 2016: a $15 minimum wage would have prevented 1,400 annual premature deaths in New York City alone. If you recognize the stories suffered in that statistic, make sure you say it with your vote."

Analyzing a Championship Speech

Let's see Block Format in action. In 2023, Tyler Luu competed in the National Finals on a bill about Qualified Immunity — a legal doctrine that protects government officials (including police officers) from being sued for actions taken while performing their duties, unless they violate "clearly established" law. The bill proposed removing this protection to increase police accountability.

Here's a breakdown of how his speech maps to the Block Format structure:

🔭
His Status Quo
De-escalation training barely required in any states + fatal police interactions frequently involve unarmed suspects
⚡
His Outcome
Action: Remove qualified immunity. Outcome: This forces departments to implement de-escalation training to reduce liability
💥
His Impact
Safer outcomes for both law enforcement and community members — fewer fatal interactions, more accountability

The Pre-Requisite

Before you dive into your block, the best debaters open with a Pre-Requisite — a brief framing statement that orients judges to the round and sets up why your argument answers the key question in the debate.

A Pre-Req typically does three things: clears up assumptions from earlier speeches, summarizes the state of the debate, and provides an overview of why your argument answers the most important issue in the round.

It's not a long section — 2-4 sentences. But it separates debaters who are just "giving a speech" from debaters who are actively controlling the narrative of the round.

Example Pre-Req (Carbon Tax bill)
"This debate has begun with the sponsor and the rest of my side saying that a Carbon Tax is needed now, while Sen. [X] leading the negation has pointed out that this tax could severely hurt Americans financially. I believe that if my side can prove to you that a Carbon Tax can be implemented without severely hurting Americans, we should pass this legislation. Let's examine how we already have systems in place that will ensure that."
