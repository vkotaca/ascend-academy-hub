Lesson 1 of 8
What "Standing Out" Actually Means

In early rounds. Cycles 1 through 3, most speeches sound the same. Debaters read their pre-written cases, hit the obvious talking points, and sit down. Judges hear similar arguments from multiple speakers and struggle to differentiate.

Standing out means being the speaker judges remember when they fill out their ballot. It doesn't require having the "best" argument or the loudest voice. Sometimes it's as simple as mastering the fundamentals, a clear SQUO, a well-evidenced outcome, and a humanized impact delivered with confidence. If you just do the basics well, you can win rounds. Being unique helps, but it starts with being solid.

Beyond the fundamentals, three things make you memorable: how you prep (researching angles others miss), how you frame (orienting judges to why your argument is the one that matters), and how you pay attention (referencing what's actually happening in the round instead of reading a canned speech).

Lesson 2 of 8
Researching to Be Noticed

Most debaters research the obvious angle on a bill, the one that shows up on the first page of Google. The minimum wage bill? Everyone preps "it helps low-wage workers" on aff and "it kills small businesses" on neg.

"Stand out" prep means understanding why the legislation exists, not just what it does. What triggered this bill? Was there a specific event, report, or failure that made this legislation necessary? When you understand the origin, you can build arguments that feel contextual and insightful rather than generic.

For example: instead of "the minimum wage hasn't kept up with inflation" (everyone says this), you could research the specific Congressional Research Service report that inspired the bill and reference it. Now you sound like someone who actually reads legislation, not just topic summaries.

The Ascend Standard
If you can find your argument's main claim on the first page of a Google search for the bill topic, so can every other debater in the room. Go deeper. Find the angle that makes judges think "I haven't heard that today."

The Power of Framing

Framing is how you tell judges what the debate is really about, and why your argument answers the most important question. A well-framed speech doesn't just make an argument; it tells judges how to evaluate the entire round.

Good framing sounds like: "The central question in this debate is whether the economic costs of this bill outweigh its benefits to workers. I'm going to prove that the benefits aren't just theoretical; they're measurable, immediate, and life-changing."

Bad framing sounds like: "I stand in firm affirmation of this bill. My argument today is about workers." That's not framing; it's just a table of contents.

Framing is especially powerful in early cycles because the debate hasn't been defined yet. The first debater to frame the round well often controls how judges evaluate every subsequent speech. Clear, well-done framing also makes your speech very easy for judges to remember, when they're filling out the ballot, they can summarize your speech in one sentence because you gave them the sentence.

Example Framing (Puerto Rico Statehood Bill)
"(Intro here)... The debate today will come down to whether 3.2 million American citizens deserve the same representation as everyone else, and whether the economic transition of immediate statehood is worth it. I'm going to prove that the cost of continued territorial status is far greater than any transition pain. That's the question this chamber needs to answer."

Lesson 4 of 8
Paying Attention in Round

The number one thing that separates debaters who place and debaters who don't: paying attention to what's actually happening in the round.

Most debaters write their speech before the round and read it regardless of what anyone else says. But judges notice when a speaker references a previous argument: "Senator Sandel on the negative mentioned that this bill would hurt small businesses, but they didn't account for the phase-in period in Section 3."

That single sentence does more for your ranking than 30 seconds of additional evidence. It shows you're listening, thinking, and contributing to the debate, not just delivering a canned performance.

Pay attention to both your side and the opposition. On your side: what has already been established well? Don't repeat it. On the opposition: what are their strongest arguments? Reference and address them.

The Sponsorship Advantage

Here's one of the most underused strategies in Congress: sponsoring a bill. Most debaters are scared to sponsor, they think it's harder, riskier, or less strategic than speaking later. They're wrong.

Sponsoring is actually one of the easiest speeches to give because you speak first, with no one to respond to, and you set the frame for the entire debate. You get to define the problem, present your solution, and establish the narrative before anyone else has a chance to speak.

Sponsors also benefit from primacy bias, judges tend to remember and favor the first speakers they hear. You're showing initiative, leadership, and confidence. In a room where ~15 debaters are fighting for the second or third speech, you walked up and claimed the first one.

There's another strategic benefit most debaters overlook: sponsoring guarantees you the best recency going into the next bill. Since you spoke first on this bill, you'll have the highest recency priority when the next bill opens, allowing you to pick your speaking position on the next piece of legislation.

A good sponsorship follows Block Format with a slight adjustment: spend a bit more time on the Status Quo (since you're establishing the entire context for the debate) and clearly reference what each section of the bill does.

Lesson 6 of 8
The Sponsorship Checklist

A strong sponsorship speech hits these elements in order:

1. Introduction: Hook the room. Tell judges and competitors why this bill matters right now.

2. Status Quo (expanded): Spend more time here than in a normal speech. You're establishing the problem for the entire debate. Give judges the context they need to evaluate every subsequent argument.

3. Bill explanation: Walk through what each section does. "Section 1 of this legislation does X. Section 2 creates Y mechanism." This is what we'll call the Action in Block Format (covered in depth in Module 2.2). It's especially important for sponsors because you're the first person explaining the bill.

4. Outcome: Prove that the bill solves the problem you outlined in the Status Quo. What world does this bill create? This is your argument, the new reality.

5. Impact: Why should judges care? Humanize it, connect it to real lives.

6. Conclusion: Urge the chamber to affirm. Restate the core argument in one sentence.

Lesson 7 of 8
Constructive vs. Canned

In early cycles, your speech should be constructive, actively building your side's advocacy and advancing the debate. The opposite of constructive is canned, a pre-written speech that could have been delivered in any round on this topic.

A constructive speech acknowledges the round. Even in Cycle 1, you can say something like: "The central tension in this debate will likely be [X vs. Y]. My argument addresses that head-on."

The key to effective early-round constructive speaking is simplicity and explanation. Your judges usually don't know much about the topic, and you can't assume they read the bill. Block Format (which we'll cover formally in Module 2.2) is extremely effective early because it's easy to follow, one argument, clearly structured, deeply explained.

As Ascend teaches: cards do not eliminate the need for a warrant. Just because you have evidence doesn't mean you've explained why it matters. Over-explaining is always better than under-explaining in early rounds.

Lesson 8 of 8
The Early-Round Mindset

Here's the mindset that top Ascend debaters bring to prelims:

You're not just giving a speech; you're building your side's case. Think about what your side needs, not just what you want to say. If the sponsor already established a strong SQUO, don't repeat it, build on it.

Every interaction is a scoring opportunity. Your speech matters. Your CX questions matter. How you carry yourself between speeches matters. Judges are watching even when you're not at the podium.

Be the debater judges want to see again. If a judge walks away thinking "I hope that person makes it to the next round," you've done your job. That means being clear, confident, engaged, and, above all, memorable.
