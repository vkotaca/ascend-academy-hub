How to construct a SQUO that provides context, proves a problem, and doubles as preemptive refutation.
Lesson 1 of 5
What the SQUO Really Does

Most debaters treat the Status Quo like background information, a paragraph of context before they get to the "real" argument. That misunderstanding is exactly why their blocks fall apart under refutation.

The SQUO is not background. It is the foundation of your entire argument. Its job is to prove that a specific problem exists in the current world, a problem that your Outcome will directly solve. If your SQUO doesn't prove a clear, specific problem, your Outcome has nothing to build on.

Here's what makes this tricky: the SQUO is unique to your argument, not the topic. Two debaters on the same minimum wage bill can run completely different SQUOs. One debater might focus on the problem of overworked Americans. Another might focus on the racial wage gap. Both are valid because each debater is proving a different problem that their specific Outcome addresses.

Think of it this way: the topic is "minimum wage." Your argument is "overworking." Your SQUO proves overworking is a crisis. Your Outcome proves the bill fixes overworking. If you wrote a SQUO about the general state of minimum wage in America, you'd have background, not an argument foundation.

Multiple Choice
Two debaters are arguing the same education reform bill. Debater A's SQUO focuses on teacher shortages. Debater B's SQUO focuses on outdated curricula. Which statement is correct?
Lesson 2 of 5
The Tagline

Every great SQUO starts with a tagline, a single sentence that captures the exact problem you're about to prove. Think of it as the thesis statement of your Status Quo section.

The tagline does two critical things: it tells judges exactly what to listen for in your evidence, and it frames the problem in your terms before anyone can reframe it in theirs. When a judge hears your tagline, they should immediately understand what your argument is about.

A strong tagline is specific, opinionated, and sets up a clear problem. Compare these two:

Weak Tagline
"The current minimum wage has been a topic of debate for many years."
Strong Tagline
"Workers, who are the backbones of companies, are being squeezed for profit."

The weak tagline is a statement of fact that doesn't commit to a problem. The strong tagline immediately establishes a specific injustice, workers are being exploited. Judges now know exactly what evidence to expect and what problem your argument will solve.

Your tagline should be memorable, assertive, and narrow. If your tagline could apply to any argument on the topic, it's too broad. If your tagline doesn't suggest a problem, it's too neutral.

Multiple Choice
A debater arguing a healthcare bill writes this tagline: "Healthcare is an important issue in the United States." What's wrong with it?
True / False

Lesson 3 of 5
Evidence Selection

After your tagline, you need evidence that proves the problem is real. This is where many debaters go wrong, they pick evidence that's interesting but doesn't actually prove their specific problem.

Your SQUO card should do one of two things: provide a statistic that quantifies the problem or cite past precedent that demonstrates the problem's pattern. One strong card is always better than two weak ones. Judges remember the strongest piece of evidence you present, and weak cards dilute the strong ones.

The key test: does this evidence prove MY argument's problem, or does it just relate to the topic?

Weak Evidence (Minimum Wage. Overworking Argument)
"The federal minimum wage has not been raised since 2009.". This is a fact about minimum wage, but it doesn't prove that workers are being overworked.
Strong Evidence (Minimum Wage. Overworking Argument)
"Ben Zipperer, Economic Policy Institute, June 2019: more than 13 million minimum wage workers have to work more than one permanent job just to afford rent.". This directly proves workers are being overworked because of low wages.

Notice the difference: both cards are about minimum wage, but only the second one proves the specific problem (overworking) that the argument is built around. The first card is background information. The second is proof.

Lesson 4 of 5
SQUO as Preemptive Refutation

Here's the advanced play that separates good debaters from championship debaters: a great SQUO doesn't just prove a problem; it makes your entire argument harder to refute.

Think about what an opponent has to do to beat your argument. Normally, they attack your Outcome: "The bill won't actually reduce overworking." But if your SQUO has already proven, with hard evidence; that overworking is a massive crisis affecting 13 million workers, the opponent now has a two-step problem:

Step 1: They must disprove that the problem exists (or show it's not that bad).
Step 2: Only THEN can they attack whether the bill fixes it.

Most opponents skip Step 1 and go straight to Step 2. When they do, your SQUO still stands unchallenged. Judges notice. The problem is proven, the solution is questioned, and judges lean toward "even if the solution isn't perfect, the problem clearly needs addressing."

This is why your SQUO evidence matters so much. A vague SQUO can be waved away. A SQUO backed by specific data forces opponents to engage with your evidence. That's a fight they often don't want to have.

Strategic Insight
The strongest SQUOs create what coaches call a "two-front war." Opponents must fight your problem AND your solution. If they only fight the solution, your problem stands and judges wonder why we wouldn't try to fix something that's clearly broken.
Multiple Choice
An opponent attacks your Outcome but never addresses your SQUO. What advantage does this give you?
True / False
A SQUO backed by vague, general claims is just as effective for preemptive refutation as one backed by specific data.
Lesson 5 of 5
Common SQUO Mistakes

Now that you know what a great SQUO looks like, let's diagnose the three most common mistakes that undermine even well-researched arguments.

Mistake 1: Too Broad. Your SQUO covers the entire topic instead of your specific problem. If your argument is about overworking, don't give judges a history of the minimum wage debate. Every sentence in your SQUO should serve your specific problem.

Mistake 2: Too Vague. Your SQUO gestures at a problem but doesn't prove it with specific data. "Many Americans struggle with low wages" is vague. "13 million minimum wage workers hold two or more jobs" is specific. Judges need numbers, sources, and concrete reality, not impressions.

Mistake 3: Too Long. Your SQUO eats into your Outcome and Impact time. The SQUO should be tight, a tagline plus one strong piece of evidence. If your SQUO is running 90 seconds in a 3-minute speech, you've left yourself no time to prove the solution works or why judges should care. The SQUO sets the table; the Outcome and Impact are the meal.
