Lesson 1 of 5
Action vs. Outcome

The Outcome section of your block actually contains two distinct parts, and confusing them is one of the most common mistakes in Congressional Debate.

The Action is a 1-2 sentence factual reference to what the bill does. It is not arguable. It is not your opinion. It is simply what the legislation mandates, funds, or prohibits. Think of it as the mechanical explanation: "Section 1 of this legislation does X. Section 2 does Y."

The Outcome is YOUR argument, the new reality the bill creates because of that action. This IS arguable. This is where opponents will attack you. The Outcome answers: "Because the bill does X, the world changes in this specific way."

The Action is context. The Outcome is the argument. Judges need the Action to understand what's happening. They evaluate the Outcome to decide if the bill is worth passing or not.

Action (factual, not arguable)
"Section 1 of this legislation raises the federal minimum wage from $7.25 to $15 per hour over a five-year phase-in period."
Outcome (your argument, arguable)
"When you double the wages of 13 million workers, most will no longer need a second job. That means millions of Americans can work one job, come home for dinner, and actually be present for their families."

Lesson 2 of 5
Referencing the Legislation

One of the fastest ways to gain credibility in a Congressional Debate round is to show judges you've actually read the bill. Many debaters talk about the topic without ever referencing the specific legislation. Judges notice.

When you read a bill, look for: the section numbers (Section 1, Section 2, etc.), the specific mechanisms (what does each section mandate, fund, or prohibit?), and the implementation details (timelines, dollar amounts, agencies involved).

The standard phrasing is direct: "Section 1 of this legislation mandates..." or "Under Section 2, the bill allocates..." You don't need to read the legal text verbatim; you're summarizing the mechanism in plain language.

Why does this matter? Because debates are about specific legislation, not abstract policy. A debater who says "this bill raises the minimum wage" sounds like they read a headline. A debater who says "Section 1 phases in a $15 federal minimum over five years, starting at $9.50 in year one" sounds like they did the work.

Pro Tip
When prepping, make sure you're very familiar with what the bill does and have the key sections written out for you to easily reference.

Lesson 3 of 5
Proving the Mechanism

This is the most critical and most often botched part of the Outcome: proving that the bill's action actually leads to the result you claim. This is what we call the link chain.

Your Outcome must logically follow from your Action. If the bill raises the minimum wage (Action), and you claim it reduces overworking (Outcome), you must show HOW higher wages lead to fewer hours worked. The link is: higher wages → workers can afford rent on one salary → workers drop their second job → overworking decreases.

Without this link, you're just asserting. "The bill raises wages, therefore overworking goes down" is a claim. "The Census Bureau shows 13 million workers hold two jobs solely to afford rent, when wages double, the financial pressure to hold two jobs disappears" is a proven mechanism.

Evidence belongs here too. Your Outcome should include at least one card that proves the mechanism works, either through data, expert analysis, or real-world precedent. If a city already raised its minimum wage and saw overworking decline, that's your strongest proof.

Weak Link (assertion without proof)
"Raising the minimum wage will reduce poverty because higher wages mean more money."
Strong Link (proven mechanism)
"A Walden University study, March 2018, found that workers averaging 11+ hours daily are 2.5 times more likely to develop depression. When wages double, most workers can afford to drop their second job. The University of Washington, October 2020, found that after Seattle's $15 minimum wage, average hours worked per person declined by 9%, meaning workers earned more while working less."

Lesson 4 of 5
The Outcome Is Your Argument

Here's the conceptual breakthrough that changes how you think about Congressional Debate: Congress ultimately comes down to a battle of outcomes. Which world is better, the world where the bill passes, or the world where the status quo continues?

Your SQUO proves the current world has a problem. Your Action explains how the bill works. But your Outcome is the argument itself; it's the new reality you're asking judges to choose.

This means your Outcome must be two things simultaneously:

Specific enough to defend. "This bill makes things better" is indefensible because it's too vague. "This bill reduces the number of Americans working two jobs by giving them a livable wage" is specific, and therefore defensible. You can point to evidence, cite data, and prove it step by step.

Broad enough to matter. "This bill saves one person $47 a week" is too narrow for judges to care about. Your Outcome should connect to a meaningful scale, millions of workers, systemic change, national impact. The sweet spot is a specific mechanism that affects a large population.

Lesson 5 of 5
Building an Airtight Link Chain

Let's put it all together. The entire Block Format is one link chain: SQUO proves problem → Action explains mechanism → Outcome proves solution. If any link is weak, the whole argument collapses.

Here's a complete walkthrough with a minimum wage bill:

🔭
SQUO (Problem)
Tagline: Workers are being overworked. It's killing them.
Evidence: Ben Zipperer, Economic Policy Institute, June 2019: more than 13 million minimum wage workers hold two or more permanent jobs just to afford rent.
⚡
Action + Outcome
Action: Section 1 raises the federal minimum wage to $15/hr over a 5-year phase-in.
Outcome: University of Washington, October 2020: after Seattle implemented a $15 minimum wage, average hours worked per person declined by 9%, workers earned more while working less. When wages double nationally, 13 million Americans can drop their second job.
💥
Impact
Evidence: Columbia University Mailman School of Public Health, March 2021: raising the minimum wage to $15 would prevent an estimated 1,400 premature deaths annually in New York City alone.
Workers who are no longer overworked are healthier, present for their families, and more productive; that's a world worth fighting for.

Notice how each link connects to the next, and each one has evidence. The SQUO doesn't talk about minimum wage broadly; it proves overworking with a specific source. The Action names the section and the amount. The Outcome cites a real study showing the mechanism works. The Impact cites a study proving the human stakes.

The test: Can an opponent break any one link? If your SQUO is proven with strong data, opponents must first disprove the problem. If your Action accurately references the bill, it's not arguable. If your Outcome is backed by evidence showing the mechanism works, opponents must provide counter-evidence. That's an airtight chain.
