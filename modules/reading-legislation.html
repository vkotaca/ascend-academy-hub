Reading & Analyzing Legislation
How to break down a bill, understand what it actually does, and identify the real debate underneath the legal language.
Lesson 1 of 8
Why Reading the Bill Matters

Most debaters skim the bill, Google the topic, and start writing arguments. This is a mistake. The bill IS the debate. Every argument you make must connect to what the legislation actually does, not just what the topic is about in general.

Judges notice when debaters reference specific sections of a bill. It signals preparation, specificity, and understanding. Debaters who argue about "Puerto Rico statehood" in the abstract lose to debaters who say "Section 3 of this bill grants Puerto Rico two Senate seats and House representation based on census data; that changes the balance of power in Congress."

Reading the bill also reveals the real debate, the specific mechanism the bill uses, which is often where affirmative and negative sides actually disagree. Two people can agree that a problem exists and still disagree about whether this particular bill solves it.

Lesson 2 of 8
Anatomy of a Bill

Every bill on your docket follows a predictable structure. Once you know the pattern, you can break down any piece of legislation quickly:

Title: The formal name of the legislation. "A Bill to Grant Puerto Rico Statehood."

Whereas clauses (Resolutions only): These explain the reasoning behind the resolution, why it's being proposed. They often contain useful context for your speeches.

Sections: The numbered parts of the bill that specify exactly what it does. Section 1 might establish a new policy. Section 2 might define the scope. Section 3 might set up the mechanism for how it works.

Enactment / Implementation: When the bill takes effect and who's responsible for carrying it out. This matters for arguments about feasibility and timeline.

These parts map to what Ascend calls Block Format, our signature argument structure. We'll cover Block Format in much greater depth in Module 2.2. For now, just know that every part of a bill feeds directly into a part of your argument.

Lesson 3 of 8
The 5 Questions to Ask Every Bill

After reading the bill, run it through these five questions. They'll give you everything you need to start building arguments:

1. What problem is this trying to solve? Every bill exists because someone believes something is wrong with the status quo. Identify that problem; it's the foundation of affirmative arguments.

2. What mechanism does the bill use to solve it? Does it regulate? Fund? Ban? Create a new agency? The mechanism matters because it's where most debates actually happen.

3. Who is affected? Identify the stakeholders, workers, businesses, states, specific demographics. Arguments become stronger when you can name who wins and who loses.

4. What could go wrong? This is the seed of negative argumentation. Does the mechanism have unintended consequences? Is it too expensive? Does it create perverse incentives?

5. Has anything like this been tried before? Precedent is powerful. If a similar policy worked (or failed) somewhere, that's evidence you can use.

The Ascend Standard
Ascend debaters don't just read a bill, they interrogate it. Every Ascend prep session starts with these five questions before anyone opens a browser. The questions give your research direction. Without them, you're Googling blindly and hoping to find something useful.

Lesson 4 of 8
Bills vs. Resolutions

Your docket will contain three types of legislation: bills, resolutions, and occasionally constitutional amendments. The distinction matters for how you argue.

Bills propose specific, enforceable actions. They have numbered sections, mechanisms, timelines, and implementation details. Arguments about bills focus on whether the specific mechanism works, feasibility, effectiveness, unintended consequences.

Resolutions express a position or principle. They use WHEREAS clauses to explain reasoning, then RESOLVED clauses to state the position. Resolutions don't create enforceable law, they send a message. Arguments about resolutions focus on whether the principle is correct and whether expressing it is productive.

Constitutional amendments propose changes to the U.S. Constitution itself. These are the highest-stakes legislation you'll debate; they're permanent, extremely difficult to reverse, and affect the fundamental framework of American government. Arguments about amendments focus on whether the change is necessary enough to justify altering the Constitution and what long-term consequences it could have.

The biggest mistake debaters make: arguing a resolution like it's a bill. If a resolution says "recognize," the debate is about whether recognizing is right and useful, not about specific enforcement mechanisms that don't exist in the text.

Here are two real pieces of legislation to compare. Notice how different they look and feel:

A Bill to Grant Puerto Rico Statehood

BE IT ENACTED BY THE CONGRESS HERE ASSEMBLED THAT:

SECTION 1. Puerto Rico shall be admitted into the United States as a state and be granted all rights, privileges, and responsibilities equal to the other states.

SECTION 2. The State of Puerto Rico shall consist of all islands and territorial waters currently under the jurisdiction of the Commonwealth of Puerto Rico.

SECTION 3. Upon statehood, Puerto Rico shall receive federal representation as follows:
  A. Two seats in the United States Senate.
  B. Representatives in the United States House determined by the latest census data.
  C. Electors based on its congressional representation.

SECTION 4. The Department of the Interior (DOI) shall oversee implementation of this legislation:
  A. All Puerto Rican residents shall be subject to federal income tax laws, and federal funding will be allocated for economic adjustments.
  B. United States federal laws will take full effect in Puerto Rico, overriding any conflicting local laws.
  C. Congress shall approve the state constitution proposed by the Legislative Assembly of Puerto Rico.

SECTION 5. This legislation shall immediately go into effect.

SECTION 6. All laws in conflict with this legislation are hereby declared null and void.

A Resolution to Recognize Taiwan as an Independent Nation

WHEREAS, The People's Republic of China continues to exert economic and military pressure on Taiwan; and

WHEREAS, Taiwan functions as a sovereign democratic nation with an independent government and economy; and

WHEREAS, Recognizing Taiwan would reaffirm United States support for democratic allies in the Indo-Pacific region; and

WHEREAS, The United States' current policy of strategic ambiguity has increased geopolitical tensions with China; now, therefore, be it

RESOLVED, That the Congress here assembled formally recognize Taiwan as an independent nation; and, be it

FURTHER RESOLVED, That the United States establish full diplomatic relations with Taiwan, including an exchange of ambassadors.

Notice the difference: the Puerto Rico bill has six numbered sections with specific mechanisms, representation details, implementation agency, tax implications. The Taiwan resolution has WHEREAS clauses explaining the reasoning and RESOLVED clauses stating the position. No enforcement mechanism. No implementation timeline. Just a formal declaration.

Pro Tip
When you see a resolution on the docket, your first instinct might be to argue about enforcement, "how will this actually work?" Resist that instinct. A resolution's power is in the message it sends, not the mechanism it creates. The debate is about whether sending that message is wise.

Lesson 5 of 8
Finding the "Real Debate"

Every piece of legislation has a surface-level debate and a real debate underneath. The surface is obvious, "should Puerto Rico become a state?" The real debate is more specific, "will immediate statehood with full federal tax obligations help or hurt Puerto Rico's economy during the transition?"

Finding the real debate means identifying where the affirmative and negative actually disagree. Often, both sides agree on the problem (Puerto Rico deserves fair representation) but disagree about the solution (does this specific bill accomplish that without causing harm?).

The debater who identifies and speaks to the real debate sounds smarter, more prepared, and more relevant than the debater who argues in generalities. This is why reading the bill's specific mechanism matters, the mechanism is where the real debate lives.

Lesson 6 of 8
Reading Between the Lines

Many bills contain strategic ambiguities, language that sounds clear but leaves room for interpretation. Recognizing these gives you powerful material for speeches. Let's look at real examples from our two pieces of legislation:

Unrealistic timelines: The Puerto Rico bill says "This legislation shall immediately go into effect." Immediate statehood is enormously complex, transitioning tax systems, integrating federal agencies, holding new elections, ratifying a state constitution. Is instant implementation realistic? That's a powerful negative argument.

Sweeping overrides: Section 4B of the Puerto Rico bill states that federal laws will take "full effect in Puerto Rico, overriding any conflicting local laws." That's a sweeping statement. Which local laws get overridden? Puerto Rico has its own civil code, tax structure, and legal traditions. What happens to all of that?

Missing enforcement: The Taiwan Resolution says "recognize" and "establish full diplomatic relations," but it doesn't specify what happens if China retaliates economically or militarily. There's no enforcement mechanism for protecting Taiwan, and no contingency plan. A resolution with no teeth is just a strongly worded letter.

Broad language: The Puerto Rico bill says federal laws will take effect "overriding any conflicting local laws", but which local laws? Puerto Rico has its own tax code, labor laws, environmental regulations, and more. The bill doesn't specify what gets overridden, and that ambiguity is debatable. Always look for sweeping language that the bill never narrows down.

Lesson 7 of 8
From Bill to Argument

Now you know how to read a bill, here's a preview of how that reading becomes an argument.

We'll formally introduce Block Format. Ascend's signature argument structure, in Module 2.2. For now, here's a preview of how bill reading maps to argument building:

The problem you identified (Question 1) → becomes your Status Quo. "The current world has this specific problem that this bill is designed to address."

The mechanism you identified (Question 2) → becomes your Action. "Section 1 of this bill does X, and Section 2 does Y."

The effect of that mechanism → becomes your Outcome. "Because of that action, the world changes in this specific way."

The stakeholders you identified (Question 3) → become your Impact. "This change matters because it affects these specific people in these specific ways."

Everything you need for a strong speech starts in the text of the bill. The research you do afterward fills in evidence, but the argument structure comes from the legislation itself.

Lesson 8 of 8
Practice: Breaking Down a Real Bill

Let's apply everything with the Puerto Rico Statehood Bill. Here it is again:

A Bill to Grant Puerto Rico Statehood

BE IT ENACTED BY THE CONGRESS HERE ASSEMBLED THAT:

SECTION 1. Puerto Rico shall be admitted into the United States as a state and be granted all rights, privileges, and responsibilities equal to the other states.

SECTION 2. The State of Puerto Rico shall consist of all islands and territorial waters currently under the jurisdiction of the Commonwealth of Puerto Rico.

SECTION 3. Upon statehood, Puerto Rico shall receive federal representation as follows:
  A. Two seats in the United States Senate.
  B. Representatives in the United States House determined by the latest census data.
  C. Electors based on its congressional representation.

SECTION 4. The Department of the Interior (DOI) shall oversee implementation of this legislation:
  A. All Puerto Rican residents shall be subject to federal income tax laws, and federal funding will be allocated for economic adjustments.
  B. United States federal laws will take full effect in Puerto Rico, overriding any conflicting local laws.
  C. Congress shall approve the state constitution proposed by the Legislative Assembly of Puerto Rico.

SECTION 5. This legislation shall immediately go into effect.

SECTION 6. All laws in conflict with this legislation are hereby declared null and void.

Running it through the 5 questions:

1. Problem: Puerto Rico is a US territory whose 3.2 million residents are US citizens but lack voting representation in Congress, can't vote in presidential elections, and receive unequal federal funding compared to states.

2. Mechanism: Full statehood, admitting Puerto Rico as the 51st state with Senate seats, House representatives, and electoral votes. The DOI oversees the transition, including imposing federal income tax and overriding conflicting local laws.

3. Affected parties: Puerto Rican residents (3.2M), existing states (representation shifts), Congress (new members), businesses in Puerto Rico (new tax obligations), and the broader US political landscape.

4. What could go wrong: Economic shock from immediate federal income tax. "Immediately go into effect" (Section 5) leaves no transition period for a massive structural change. Section 4B overrides all conflicting local laws, what laws get swept away? Political implications of two new Senate seats.

5. Precedent: Hawaii and Alaska were admitted as states in 1959. Both had transition periods. Puerto Rico has held multiple referenda on statehood, the most recent in 2020 showed 52% support.

Pro Tip
Notice what Section 5 says: "immediately go into effect." Statehood is one of the most complex structural changes imaginable, new tax systems, new representation, overriding local laws. The word "immediately" is a goldmine for the negation. Meanwhile, the affirmation can argue that delay perpetuates inequality. One word, two strong arguments.
