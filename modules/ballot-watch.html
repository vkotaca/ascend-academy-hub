Reading the Ballot
What judges actually write down, what they reward, and how to improve even from bare ballots.
Lesson 1 of 5
What Judges Write Down

After each round, judges submit ballots with rankings and (sometimes) comments about each speaker. Understanding what judges actually write — and what they don't — is essential for interpreting feedback correctly.

Here's the reality: most ballots are sparse. Judges are evaluating 12-16 speakers in a 2-hour round, often writing while the next speaker is already talking. They don't have time for detailed analysis of every speech.

A typical ballot comment is 1-3 sentences per speaker. It usually includes:

The argument topic: "Spoke aff on the healthcare bill about rural hospital access." This helps the judge remember who said what when finalizing rankings.

One strength: "Clear structure," "Strong evidence from CBO," "Engaged with previous speakers." This is what stood out positively.

Sometimes one area for improvement: "Could slow down delivery," "Argument needed more impact analysis," "Didn't engage with the debate." Not all ballots include this.

Don't be discouraged by brief comments. Sparse ballots are normal — they don't mean the judge wasn't paying attention. The ranking itself is the primary data. Comments are a bonus when you get them.

If a judge's ballot only has 1-2 sentences about your speech, it likely means they weren't paying close attention to you.
Lesson 2 of 5
What Judges Reward

After reading thousands of ballots, clear patterns emerge in what judges consistently praise and consistently criticize. Knowing these patterns lets you optimize for what actually drives rankings.

Common praise (what gets high ranks):

"Clear argument" — The judge understood your point immediately. Structure wins. A well-organized SQUO-Outcome-Impact block is easier to follow than a brilliant but disorganized stream of consciousness.

"Engaged with the debate" — You referenced other speakers, responded to opposing arguments, or built on points already made. This is the single most common praise on top-ranked ballots.

"Strong evidence" — You cited credible, recent, specific sources. Not just "studies show" — actual organizations, dates, and numbers.

"Compelling delivery" — Your energy, pace, and presence made the speech engaging to listen to, not just informative.

Common criticism (what gets low ranks):

"Didn't engage with others" — You gave an isolated speech that could have been delivered in an empty room. This is the most common reason for mid-pack rankings.

"Rushed" — Too much content crammed into too little time. Quality of delivery was sacrificed for quantity of arguments.

"Argument unclear" — The judge couldn't follow the logic. Usually a structure problem, not an intelligence problem.

Lesson 3 of 5
Reading Between the Lines

Judges are diplomatic. They rarely write harsh criticism directly — instead, they use coded language that you need to learn to decode. Understanding what judges really mean helps you identify your actual weaknesses.

Here's a translation guide:

"Good delivery" — Often means content was weak. If a judge only praises your delivery, it probably means your argument or evidence wasn't strong enough to comment on. They noticed HOW you spoke because WHAT you said didn't stand out.

"Interesting argument" — Often means delivery needs work. The judge found your idea compelling on paper but felt it wasn't presented effectively. The argument was better than the execution.

"Nice job" — This is the participation trophy of ballot comments. It usually means the judge couldn't identify anything specifically memorable about your speech, either positive or negative. You were forgettable but inoffensive.

"Consider slowing down" — Means you were significantly too fast. Judges only write this when the pacing genuinely impaired their ability to follow your argument. It's a bigger problem than it sounds.

"Good start" — Often means the speech fell apart after a strong opening. The judge was engaged initially but lost the thread somewhere in the middle or end.

The general principle: when a judge praises one element, look at what they DIDN'T mention. The silence often tells you more than the words.

Lesson 4 of 5
Learning from Bare Ballots

Sometimes you get a ballot with nothing but a ranking — no comments at all. This is frustrating but more common than you'd like. You can still learn from bare ballots if you know how to analyze the data.

The key is to look at patterns across multiple rounds and judges:

Compare rankings across rounds. If you consistently rank 4th-6th across three different judges in three different rounds, you have a reliable data point about your current level. One bad round could be a tough draw. Consistent mid-pack placement points to a real, addressable issue.

Look for round-specific variation. If you ranked 2nd in Round 1 (where you spoke early) but 7th in Round 3 (where you spoke late), the pattern might indicate that your late-round speaking needs work. Context matters.

Compare bill-by-bill performance. If you consistently rank well on domestic policy bills but poorly on foreign policy bills, you've found a preparation gap — not a skills gap.

Track the trend over the season. Your ranking in September vs. January vs. April tells a story. Improvement — even slow improvement — means your practice is working. Stagnation means something needs to change.

Lesson 5 of 5
The Improvement Loop

The debaters who improve fastest aren't the ones who try to fix everything at once. They follow a disciplined improvement loop that compounds over the course of a season.

Here's the process, repeated after every tournament:

Step 1: Read all ballots. Don't just glance at rankings — read every comment carefully. Look for words and phrases that appear on multiple ballots from different judges.

Step 2: Identify ONE recurring theme. If three judges mention "didn't engage with other speakers," that's your theme. If two mention "rushed delivery," that's your theme. Find the single issue that appears most often across all your feedback.

Step 3: Work on it for two weeks. Focus exclusively on that one issue at every practice session. If it's engagement, practice incorporating references to previous speakers into every practice speech. If it's pacing, record yourself and measure your words-per-minute.

Step 4: Evaluate at the next tournament. Did the feedback change? Did that specific criticism disappear? If yes, move to the next most common issue. If no, adjust your approach and try again.

Don't fix everything at once. Trying to improve delivery, evidence, structure, engagement, and presence simultaneously means you improve at none of them. One improvement per tournament cycle, compounded over a season, produces extraordinary results. By your 8th tournament, you've systematically addressed 8 different weaknesses — and each one built on the last.

The Compound Formula
One improvement per tournament. Eight tournaments per season. After one season, you've systematically eliminated your eight biggest weaknesses. The debater who does this for two seasons has addressed sixteen distinct issues — and they're virtually unrecognizable from where they started.
