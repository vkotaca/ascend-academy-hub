Lesson 1 of 5
From Notes to Blocks

You've done your research. You have a stack of evidence cards — SQUO cards, Outcome cards, Impact cards — each labeled with source, date, and block assignment. Now comes the crucial transition: turning that raw material into a structured argument.

Think of your evidence cards as building materials. A pile of bricks isn't a house. You need a blueprint. That blueprint is your block structure: SQUO, Outcome, Impact — in that order, each flowing logically into the next.

Start by laying out all your cards for one argument. Group them by block:

SQUO pile: Which cards prove the current problem? Pick the 1-2 strongest. You don't need five statistics saying the same thing — you need one devastating fact and one supporting detail.

Outcome pile: Which cards prove the bill's mechanism works? This is where most debaters are weakest. If you don't have strong Outcome evidence, go back and research more before building the block.

Impact pile: Which cards show why the outcome matters? Choose the ones that connect to real human lives, not abstract economic projections.

Once you've selected your strongest cards for each block, you have the skeleton of your argument. The next step is writing taglines that connect them.

Example: Mapping Evidence for a Climate Bill
Bill: Invests $50 billion in renewable energy infrastructure. SQUO cards: U.S. CO2 emissions data, fossil fuel dependency statistics, climate damage cost projections. Best pick: "The EPA reports the U.S. emits 5.2 billion metric tons of CO2 annually, 25% from electricity generation." Outcome cards: Studies on renewable energy job creation, grid reliability after renewable transitions. Best pick: "A 2024 Department of Energy report found that states investing in renewable infrastructure reduced grid emissions by 40% within five years." Impact cards: Health effects of air pollution, climate migration projections. Best pick: "The American Lung Association estimates fossil fuel pollution causes 100,000 premature deaths annually in the U.S."

Lesson 2 of 5
Writing Your Taglines

A tagline is the one-sentence thesis that opens each block of your speech. It tells the judge exactly what you're about to prove before you prove it. Write your taglines FIRST — before you write the rest of the block.

Why? Because the tagline forces clarity. If you can't summarize your argument in one sentence, you don't understand your argument well enough to deliver it. The tagline is your north star — everything in the block must support it.

A strong tagline has three qualities:

1. It makes a claim. Not a question, not a topic announcement — a claim. "The current minimum wage forces millions into poverty" is a claim. "I'm going to talk about the minimum wage" is not.

2. It's specific. "This bill helps people" is vague. "This bill lifts 3.7 million workers above the poverty line" is specific. Specificity signals to judges that you have evidence to back it up.

3. It previews the mechanism. The best taglines hint at HOW your point works. "By eliminating mandatory minimums, this bill restores judicial discretion and reduces recidivism" tells judges the claim AND the mechanism in one sentence.

Weak Tagline
"The status quo has problems with education that this bill addresses."
Strong Tagline
"Right now, 53% of American students attend schools that can't afford updated textbooks — this bill's $30 billion in Title I funding closes that gap within three years."

Lesson 3 of 5
Proving the Outcome

The Outcome block is where most cases live or die. SQUO is usually straightforward — the problem is well-documented. Impact flows naturally from a proven outcome. But the Outcome itself — proving the bill's mechanism actually works — is the hardest part of case construction.

The most common mistake: proving the problem exists but never proving the solution works. "Millions lack healthcare" is a SQUO fact, not an Outcome argument. The Outcome must show that THIS BILL'S specific mechanism will produce the desired result.

The strongest way to prove an outcome is past precedent. Has this mechanism been tried before? In another state? In another country? What happened?

For example, if a bill proposes a carbon tax, your best Outcome evidence is what happened in British Columbia when they implemented a carbon tax in 2008. Did emissions go down? By how much? What were the side effects? Real-world precedent is the gold standard of Outcome proof because it shows the mechanism working in practice, not just in theory.

If direct precedent doesn't exist, use analogous evidence — a similar mechanism in a related context. If no country has tried your exact policy, find the closest parallel and explain why the results would transfer.

Past Precedent: The Power Move
Bill: Universal background checks for firearm purchases. Outcome proof using precedent: "Connecticut implemented universal background checks in 1995. A 2015 Johns Hopkins study found that Connecticut's firearm homicide rate dropped 40% in the decade following implementation, while states without such laws saw no comparable decline." This is devastating evidence because it's not theoretical — it already happened, it was studied by a credible institution, and the results are specific and measurable.

Lesson 4 of 5
Stress-Testing Your Argument

Before you step into the chamber, you need to break your own argument. Play devil's advocate. If you can poke holes in your own case, an opponent definitely will — and it's better to find the weaknesses now than on the floor.

Stress-testing means asking yourself three questions about every block:

1. "What would the other side say?" If you're running an aff argument on a minimum wage bill, a neg debater might argue that small businesses will cut jobs. Do you have evidence that addresses this? If not, you have a gap.

2. "Is my link chain airtight?" Does your SQUO logically lead to your Outcome? Does your Outcome logically lead to your Impact? If there's a gap — if you're claiming a result without proving the mechanism that produces it — your opponent will exploit it.

3. "Is my evidence the strongest available?" If you're citing a 2018 study and a 2024 study exists on the same topic, you're vulnerable. Opponents can dismiss outdated evidence. Always check for newer, better sources.

The best debaters don't just find weaknesses — they fix them. If your argument has a vulnerability, either strengthen it with additional evidence or build a preemptive response into your speech. Acknowledging and addressing a counterargument before your opponent raises it is one of the most powerful rhetorical moves in debate.

Stress-Test in Action: Foreign Policy Bill
Your argument: A bill increasing foreign aid to Sub-Saharan Africa will reduce regional instability. Devil's advocate test: "Opponents will say foreign aid often gets captured by corrupt governments and never reaches the intended recipients." Fix: Research USAID accountability mechanisms and find evidence of successful aid programs with anti-corruption safeguards. Build it into your block: "And before the opposition claims this aid will be wasted, the bill specifically mandates third-party auditing — a mechanism that USAID's 2023 Inspector General report found reduces aid misallocation by 60%."

Lesson 5 of 5
The Finished Product

Let's walk through the entire process from bill to finished block, using a bill that expands Pell Grants to cover vocational and technical training programs.

Step 1 — Read the bill: The bill expands Pell Grant eligibility to students in accredited vocational programs (welding, nursing, IT certification). Currently, Pell Grants only cover traditional 4-year and 2-year degree programs.

Step 2 — Research using mechanism keywords: Search "Pell Grant vocational training outcomes," "short-term credential employment rates," and "skills gap manufacturing workforce." These mechanism searches find evidence that generic "education funding" searches would miss.

Step 3 — Organize cards:

📄
SQUO Card
Georgetown CEW, 2023: 70% of jobs in growing industries require post-secondary credentials, but only 30% of Pell recipients can use grants for vocational programs that lead to those jobs.
📄
Outcome Card
Dept. of Education pilot study, 2024: States that extended financial aid to short-term credential programs saw a 45% increase in program enrollment and 78% job placement within 6 months of completion.
📄
Impact Card
Brookings Institution, 2023: Workers with vocational credentials earn on average $15,000 more annually than those with only a high school diploma, with lower student debt than 4-year degree holders.

Step 4 — Write taglines: SQUO tagline: "The current Pell Grant system locks 7 million vocational students out of the financial aid they need to enter the workforce." Outcome tagline: "Expanding eligibility works — pilot programs show 78% job placement within six months." Impact tagline: "This bill gives working-class Americans a path to the middle class without crushing student debt."

Step 5 — Stress-test: Devil's advocate: "Opponents will argue this dilutes Pell Grant funding for traditional students." Fix: The bill includes an additional $5 billion appropriation specifically for vocational expansion — it doesn't redirect existing funds. Build this preemptive rebuttal into the Outcome block.

The result: a complete, evidence-backed, stress-tested block ready for delivery. This is what turns raw research into a championship-level case.
