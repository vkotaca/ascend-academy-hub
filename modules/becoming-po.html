Becoming a Presiding Officer
How to get elected, run your procedure speech, manage the chamber, handle motions, and be a judge's best friend — the complete guide to presiding.
Lesson 1 of 8
Why Preside?

Presiding is one of the most underrated competitive advantages in Congressional Debate. Many students avoid it because it seems intimidating — but that's exactly why it's so valuable.

POs are scored separately from debaters. Many tournaments give PO awards, and a strong PO performance can earn you rankings even when speaking slots are limited. In prelim rounds where getting recognized is competitive, presiding guarantees you're doing something scoreable for the entire round.

Beyond scoring, presiding builds skills that translate directly to debate performance: procedural knowledge, composure under pressure, and the ability to command a room. Judges notice when a PO is confident and competent — and those same judges are often the ones ranking your speeches later.

Lesson 2 of 8
Getting Elected

Before you can preside, you have to win the PO election. Here's how it works:

At the start of the round, the adult moderator (or temporary chair) asks for PO candidates. Each candidate gives a brief procedure speech explaining how they'll run the chamber. The chamber then votes, and the candidate with the most votes wins.

Unless you're a known PO, getting elected in later rounds is harder — students tend to re-elect people who've done it well before. That's why prelim rounds are your best opportunity to establish yourself as a PO.

Your procedure speech is your pitch. Most procedure speeches are boring — students just list the rules. The ones that win elections sound confident, show personality, and make the chamber believe you'll run things smoothly.

Pro Tip
Don't just recite rules in your procedure speech. Tell the chamber what your priorities are: "I'll keep us moving efficiently, make sure everyone gets a fair chance to speak, and I'll be transparent about precedence at all times." Judges are watching this speech too — it's your first impression.

Lesson 3 of 8
The Procedure Speech

Your procedure speech should cover the essential rules while demonstrating confidence and leadership. Here's what to include:

Speech timing: "Speeches will be 3 minutes with a 30-second grace period. I'll tap the gavel once at 30 seconds remaining, twice at time, and three times at the end of the grace period."

Speaker recognition: "I'll recognize speakers based on precedence first, then recency. I'll maintain a visible sheet so everyone can track where they stand."

CX: "After each speech, I'll open the floor for one question from the opposing side. The questioner will have 30 seconds."

Motions: "All motions require a second. I'll handle motions in the order they're raised."

Keep it under 2 minutes. Be conversational, not robotic. Make eye contact with the chamber. End with something like: "My goal is to run a clean, fair, efficient round. Let's have a great debate."

Sample Procedure Speech Closing
"I'll recognize speakers by precedence first, then recency. I'll keep a visible tracking sheet so there's no confusion. Speeches are 3 minutes with a 30-second grace. I'll tap once at 30 seconds left, twice at time, and three times at grace. My goal is to make this round run smoothly so you can focus on debating. Let's get started."

Running the Docket

Once elected, your first job is managing the docket election. If there's no preset docket, you'll run a vote to determine the order of legislation.

The typical process: ask if anyone wants to propose a docket order. If multiple proposals are made, vote on each one. If no one proposes, you can suggest going in the order listed and ask for a vote.

After the docket is set, you call for authorship speeches on the first bill. If the original author isn't present, call for sponsors. If no one wants to sponsor, call for the first affirmative speech.

From here, debate follows the alternating pattern: affirmative, negative, affirmative, negative — with CX after each speech. Your job is to keep this flow moving smoothly while tracking who has spoken and who has priority.

The PO Sheet

The PO sheet is your command center. It's where you track every speech and question throughout the round — and it's what you use to make fair recognition decisions.

Your sheet should track for each member: name, number of speeches, number of questions, and the order in which they last spoke or questioned. This lets you quickly determine precedence and recency when multiple people stand for recognition.

Many POs write this on a large sheet visible to the chamber. Transparency builds trust — when competitors can see the sheet, they're less likely to challenge your recognition decisions.

Keep your sheet updated in real time. Don't wait until after a speech to record it — mark it as soon as you recognize a speaker. Falling behind on your sheet is the fastest way to lose control of the round.

Sample PO Sheet (mid-round snapshot)
Name             Speeches   Questions   Last Spoke
Alex M.           1             0               Speech #3
Blake T.          1             1               Speech #5
Casey R.         0             1               —
Dana K.           1             0               Speech #7
→ Next recognition priority: Casey (0 speeches), then Alex (spoke earlier than Blake)

Handling Motions

Motions are where most new POs get tripped up. Here's the standard process:

1. Member makes a motion. "I move for the Previous Question." You respond: "Is there a second?" Someone says "Second." Now it's a live motion.

2. Vote on the motion. "All those in favor of moving to the Previous Question, please raise your hand." Count hands. "All those opposed?" Count again. Announce the result: "The motion passes/fails."

3. If it passes, execute it. For Previous Question: move to a vote on the bill. For Lay on the Table: set aside the bill and move to the next item. For Recess: announce the break length.

The most common motion you'll handle is Previous Question — usually after everyone with first precedence has spoken on a bill. Expect this motion after 6-10 speeches.

Some tournaments have specific rules about when motions can be made (e.g., not during a speech, requires equal number of aff/neg speeches first). Know your tournament's rules before the round.

Lesson 7 of 8
Being a Judge's Best Friend

Here's the secret to great PO scores: the judge determines your ranking, so make their job as easy as possible.

Announce speakers clearly: "The chair recognizes [Name] for a 3-minute affirmative speech." This helps judges log who's speaking without having to ask.

Keep time visibly — hold up fingers or use clear gavel taps so judges know exactly when a speech started and ended.

Maintain composure even when things go wrong. If a motion gets confusing, slow down and work through it step by step. If a member challenges your ruling, stay calm and explain your reasoning. Judges reward POs who handle chaos gracefully far more than POs who never encounter problems.

As Ascend coach Quest Sandel puts it: even if you make a mistake — which will happen — be confident through it. Judges are more forgiving of procedural errors than they are of a PO who looks flustered and unsure.

Quest Sandel, Ascend Academy
"The judge determines if you do well or not — pander to them. Announce speakers clearly so judges can log names. Keep time visibly so they know when speeches start and end. Be their assistant, not just the chamber's traffic cop."

Common PO Mistakes & Tips

Here are the most common mistakes new POs make — and how to avoid them:

Falling behind on the PO sheet. Update it in real time. If you lose track of precedence, you'll make unfair recognition decisions and lose the chamber's trust.

Speaking too quietly. You're running a room of 20 people. Project your voice. Use the gavel to get attention, not your vocal cords.

Getting flustered by motions. If you're not sure about a motion, it's okay to pause and think. Say "Give me one moment" — that's infinitely better than making a wrong call quickly.

Forgetting to alternate sides. Always alternate aff/neg. If three people stand on the same side, recognize the one from the side that hasn't spoken yet.

Not managing time transitions. After CX ends, don't let the chamber sit in silence. Immediately call for the next speaker: "The floor is now open for a [aff/neg] speech."
