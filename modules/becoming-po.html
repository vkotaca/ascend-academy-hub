Becoming a Presiding Officer
How to get elected, run your procedure speech, manage the chamber, handle motions, and be a judge's best friend. The complete guide to presiding.
Lesson 1 of 8
Why Preside?

Presiding isn't for everyone. That's okay. Some debaters prefer to focus entirely on speaking. But if you're open to it, POing can be a strong addition to your competitive toolkit.

POs are scored separately from debaters. Many tournaments give PO awards, and a strong PO performance can earn you rankings even when speaking slots are limited. In crowded prelim rounds where getting recognized is tough, presiding guarantees you're doing something scoreable for the entire session.

Beyond scoring, presiding builds skills that transfer directly to debate performance: procedural knowledge, composure under pressure, and the ability to command a room. Judges notice when a PO is confident and competent, and those same judges are often the ones ranking your speeches later.

That said, if you'd rather spend every round speaking, that's a perfectly valid strategy too. POing is an option, not a requirement.

Lesson 2 of 8
Getting Elected

Before you can preside, you need to get elected. Here's how it works:

At the start of the round, the parliamentarian (or temporary chair) asks for PO candidates. If there's only one candidate, they're automatically appointed — no vote needed. If there are multiple candidates, each gives a brief election speech (30 seconds to 1 minute) explaining why the chamber should vote for them, then the chamber votes. If no one volunteers, the chamber generally waits until someone steps up.

Important: the election speech and the procedure speech are two different things. The election speech is your pitch to get voted in: it's short, confident, and highlights why you're the right person. The procedure speech comes after you're elected, when you explain the rules and how you'll run things.

In your election speech: list any notable PO experience you have (tournaments, rounds, awards). If this is your first time, be honest about it. Say you've been studying procedure and you're ready. Then tell them what kind of chamber you'll run. Keep it brief, confident, and personable. Crack a joke if you can. It shows personality and makes people want to vote for you.

One more thing: building relationships outside the round helps. Congress is a social event. Talk to people between rounds, get to know competitors from other schools. When election time comes, the students who know you are more likely to support you. It's not about politics; it's about trust.

Pro Tip
Your election speech should be something like: "I'll run a fast, fair, and efficient chamber with the goal of maximizing the opportunities that everyone has to speak." That's it: clear priorities, under 30 seconds. Save the procedural details for the procedure speech after you're elected.

The Procedure Speech

Once you're elected, you deliver your procedure speech — this is where you explain to the chamber exactly how you'll run things. It's different from the election speech: this is procedural, not persuasive. You've already won. Now you're informing.

Cover three things:

Speech timing: Explain gavel taps clearly: when each tap happens and what happens at grace period.

Speaker recognition: Precedence first, then recency, with a visible tracking sheet.

CX: How you'll run cross-examination and the time per questioner.

Important: If the tournament you're competing at has any rules that differ from the standard (different grace periods, CX format, motion thresholds, etc.), make that very clear to the round in your procedure speech. Don't assume everyone knows.

Keep it under 1 minute. Be conversational, not robotic. Make eye contact with the chamber. End with something like: "My goal is to run a fast, fair, efficient round. Let's have a great debate."

Sample Procedure Speech
"Hey everyone. Speeches will be 3 minutes with a 10-second grace period — I'll tap once two minutes into your speech, twice at two minutes 30 seconds, three times at 2 minutes 55 seconds, and then I'll start gaveling you down from 3 minutes onward and verbally cut you off at 3 minutes and 10 seconds. I'll recognize speakers by precedence first, then recency, and I'll keep a visible sheet so everyone can see where they stand. After each speech I'll open up CX with 30 seconds per questioner. I'll give you one tap after 20 seconds and two taps at 30 seconds. My goal is to run a fast, fair, efficient round. Let's have a great debate."

Running the Round

Once elected, your first job is managing the docket election. If there's no preset docket, you'll run a vote to determine the order of legislation.

The typical process: ask if anyone wants to propose a docket order. If multiple proposals are made, vote on each one. If no one proposes, you can suggest going in the order listed and ask for a vote.

After the docket is set, you call for authorship speeches on the first bill. If the original author isn't present, call for sponsors. If no one wants to sponsor, call for the first affirmative speech.

From here, debate follows the alternating pattern: affirmative, negative, affirmative, negative, with CX after each speech. Your job is to keep this flow moving smoothly while tracking who has spoken and who has priority.

The PO Sheet

The PO sheet is your command center. It's where you track every speech and question throughout the round. It's what you use to make fair recognition decisions.

Your sheet should track for each member: name, number of speeches, number of questions, and the order in which they last spoke or questioned. This lets you quickly determine precedence and recency when multiple people stand for recognition.

Many POs write this on a large sheet visible to the chamber. Transparency builds trust: when competitors can see the sheet, they're less likely to challenge your recognition decisions.

Keep your sheet updated in real time. Don't wait until after a speech to record it. Mark it as soon as you recognize a speaker. Falling behind on your sheet is the fastest way to lose control of the round.

Handling Motions

Motions are where most new POs get tripped up. Here's the standard process:

1. Member makes a motion. "I move for the Previous Question." You respond: "Is there a second?" Someone says "Second." Now it's a live motion.

2. Vote on the motion. "All those in favor of moving to the Previous Question, please raise your hand." Count hands. "All those opposed?" Count again. Announce the result: "The motion passes/fails."

3. If it passes, execute it. For Previous Question: move to a vote on the bill. For Lay on the Table: set aside the bill and move to the next item. For Recess: announce the break length.

One of the most common motions you'll handle is Previous Question — it ends debate on the current bill and triggers a vote. Expect this motion after roughly 10–12 speeches on a bill.

Important: while most motions require a second, certain motions are at the PO's discretion. Most notably, a Point of Personal Privilege (e.g., a member asking to exit the chamber) doesn't need a second; you can grant it on the spot.

Being a Judge's Best Friend

Here's the secret to great PO scores: the judge determines your ranking, so make their job as easy as possible.

Announce speakers clearly: "The chair recognizes [Name] for an affirmative speech." This helps judges log who's speaking without having to ask.

Keep time visibly: use clear and consistent gavel taps so judges know exactly when a speech ends.

Maintain composure even when things go wrong. If a motion gets confusing, slow down and work through it step by step. If a member challenges your ruling, stay calm and explain your reasoning. Judges reward POs who handle chaos gracefully far more than POs who never encounter problems.

Lesson 8 of 8
Common PO Mistakes & Tips

Here are the most common mistakes new POs make, and how to avoid them:

Falling behind on the PO sheet. Update it in real time. If you lose track of precedence, you'll make unfair recognition decisions and lose the chamber's trust.

Speaking too quietly. You're running a room of ~15 people. Project your voice. Use the gavel to get attention, not your vocal cords.

Getting flustered by motions. If you're not sure about a motion, it's okay to pause and think. Say "Give me one moment." That's infinitely better than making a wrong call quickly.

Not managing time transitions. After CX ends, don't let the chamber sit in silence. Immediately call for the next speaker: "The floor is now open for a [aff/neg] speech."
