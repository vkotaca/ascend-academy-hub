The Anatomy of an Argument
Whether you're in a debate round, writing an essay, or bargaining for a later curfew, every good argument has the same four parts. Master them here.
Lesson 1 of 6
What Makes a Good Argument?

You've probably made hundreds of arguments in your life without thinking about it: convincing a friend to watch your show, persuading a teacher to extend a deadline, or debating whether pineapple belongs on pizza. But in competitive debate, "trust me, it's good" doesn't cut it.

Every strong argument follows the same universal structure called CWEI:

  • C — Claim (your position)
  • W — Warrant (your reasoning)
  • E — Evidence (your proof)
  • I — Impact (why it matters)

If any one of these is missing, your argument has a hole, and in debate, your opponents will find it. The rest of this module teaches you how to build each piece so your arguments are airtight.

Lesson 2 of 6
The Claim

A claim is your one-sentence thesis — a clear statement of your position on a topic. It's the anchor of your entire argument. Everything that follows exists to support this sentence.

Good claims are debatable. That means someone could reasonably disagree with you. If no one would argue against it, it's not a claim; it's a fact.

Strong Claims vs. Weak Claims
Strong: "Homework should be banned in public schools."
Strong: "Solar panels are the most effective way to combat climate change."
Weak: "Climate change exists." (This is a fact, not debatable.)
Weak: "Some people like homework." (This is an observation, not a position.)

In Congressional Debate, your claim is the core position of your speech. It should be specific enough to argue deeply and clear enough that a judge can repeat it back from memory. If a judge can't summarize your claim in one sentence after your speech, you've already lost ground.

True / False
"The U.S. Capitol building is located in Washington, D.C." is a strong claim for a debate argument.
Lesson 3 of 6
The Warrant

A warrant is the logical reasoning behind your claim — the explanation of why what you're asserting is true. Think of it as the "because" statement.

Your claim says what you believe. Your warrant says why you believe it. Without a warrant, you're just stating an opinion and expecting people to take your word for it.

Claims + Warrants
"Homework should be banned because students experience more stress without seeing actual educational benefits."

"Solar panels are the best way to combat climate change because they're the most accessible and scalable form of renewable energy."

Notice how the warrant gives the judge a logical reason to believe your claim. It connects the dots. In debate, judges won't rank you well just because you made an assertion. They need to understand the reasoning behind it. A strong warrant makes your argument feel inevitable rather than arbitrary.

Lesson 4 of 6
Evidence & Data

You have a claim and a warrant, but let's face it, you're a student. An adult judging you, or anyone listening, needs to know that what you're saying is backed up by experts. That's where evidence comes in.

Evidence is outside proof that supports your claim and warrant. It comes from credible sources — academic journals, government reports, research publications, news organizations. It usually takes one of two forms:

  • Statistics — numbers, percentages, data points that quantify your argument
  • Past precedent — examples of when something similar happened before, proving your reasoning works in the real world
What Makes Evidence Credible?
Good sources: Stanford University research, Congressional Budget Office reports, The Brookings Institution, peer-reviewed journals, major news outlets.

Weak sources: Random blogs, opinion pieces without data, Wikipedia (as a primary source), social media posts.

A judge hearing "According to a 2024 Stanford study..." instantly trusts your argument more than "I read somewhere that..."

Lesson 5 of 6
The Impact

You've made your claim, explained your reasoning, and backed it up with evidence. Now comes the part that wins rounds: the impact.

An impact answers the question: why does your argument matter? How are real people affected? Is their life getting better or getting worse? This is where you make judges feel the stakes of your argument, not just understand them logically.

If you're arguing for something (affirmation), your impact shows why the outcome is good for people. If you're arguing against something (negation), your impact shows why the outcome is bad for people.

Impact in Action
Claim: Homework should be banned.
Warrant: Students are more stressed and don't see educational benefits.
Evidence: Stanford research showing homework beyond 2 hours correlates with higher stress and lower performance.
Impact: Reducing this mental strain means students are happier, healthier, and more eager to learn. When we remove the source of burnout, students actually want to engage with content, leading to better educational outcomes and better lives.

Notice how the impact doesn't just say "this is good." It explains how people's lives change: happier, healthier, more eager to learn. That emotional connection is what separates a decent argument from one that sticks with judges.

Lesson 6 of 6
Putting It All Together

Let's see a complete CWEI argument in action. Imagine you're debating a bill to ban single-use plastics:

Full CWEI Example
Claim: The United States should ban single-use plastics nationwide.

Warrant: Single-use plastics are the largest contributor to ocean pollution and take over 400 years to decompose, meaning every piece ever produced still exists in some form today.

Evidence: According to a 2023 report from the UN Environment Programme, over 11 million metric tons of plastic enter the ocean annually, and single-use items account for roughly 50% of all plastic waste globally.

Impact: Banning single-use plastics would protect marine ecosystems that billions of people depend on for food and livelihood. Coastal communities — particularly in developing nations — would see cleaner waters, healthier fisheries, and reduced healthcare costs from microplastic contamination.

See how each piece builds on the last? The claim states the position. The warrant explains why. The evidence proves it with data. And the impact makes you care about real people.
