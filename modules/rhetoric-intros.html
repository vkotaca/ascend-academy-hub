Lesson 1 of 6
What Is Rhetoric?

Everything in Units 2–4 was about what to say — how to build arguments, deploy them strategically, and research effectively. This unit is about how to say it.

Rhetoric is probably the biggest thing that separates Congressional Debate from every other debate event. And it's the part that makes this event real.

Here's the truth: in the real world, you can't just read research papers at people and expect them to be convinced. You have to persuade with words. That's rhetoric — not fancy vocabulary or flowery language, but words that actually mean something to people.

In debate, rhetoric means telling a story with your argument. You're using vivid imagery, descriptive details, and plain language to describe what the world looks like after the legislation has been enacted. Remember: legislation is about change. Your job is to describe that change and how it affects people's lives.

The Key Insight
Great rhetoric isn't about sounding impressive. It's about taking your evidence and warrant and summarizing them into one or two sentences that anyone can understand — then pushing your advocacy forward by focusing on the people affected or the change enacted. That's it.

Lesson 2 of 6
Rhetoric Within Blocks

The most common place you'll use rhetoric is inside your argument blocks — after your evidence, before moving on. The formula is simple:

  1. Summarize your evidence and warrant in one or two plain sentences
  2. Push advocacy forward by focusing on the people affected or the change enacted
  3. Move on. Don't belabor it.

That's the entire formula. You're not writing a novel — you're adding one or two lines that translate data into human terms. The biggest mistake beginners make is overthinking it. They compare themselves to great orators and freeze. But the best rhetoric is simple, direct, and grounded in what you just argued.

Don't Chase the Perfect Line
Looking for the perfect line to start your speech — or the perfect line period — is the worst way to find it. It's never going to come to you by sitting and thinking. Put pen to paper (or cursor to Google Doc) and just start writing. The perfect line is something that gets built over time as you write, cut, and chisel.

Lesson 3 of 6
Rhetoric in Practice

Let's see this in action with a real case — a bill for universal pre-K. Watch how rhetoric transforms each block from dry data into something judges feel.

Status Quo Block
The argument: "Child care expenses amount to 35% of low-income families' earnings."

The rhetoric: "Back in the good old days, kids raised themselves. But now, families need someone to look after their children — and doing so takes up more money than they can afford to spend."

One sentence. A touch of humor to keep judges engaged, then straight to the point — child care is too expensive. Summarize and move on.
Action/Outcome Block
The argument: "Pre-K boosts social and emotional development and cognitive abilities for low-income children."

The rhetoric: "It's obviously hard for a four-year-old to understand that 2 plus 2 equals 4. But what has long-term benefits are the social skills they gain along the way — understanding friendship, sympathy, and teamwork. Those are skills children can only get by interacting with other children. Having a positive start in early childhood means children are more likely to view school as a positive thing, not just somewhere to kill six hours. The trajectory of their lives can be transformed when we invest in them from day one."

Notice: nothing fancy. He's just describing what the evidence means in human terms — what a kid's life actually looks like when pre-K works.

The pattern is the same every time: take your data, describe what it looks like for real people, and move on. Are these lines perfect? No. But they're written, they're clear, and they connect judges to the stakes of the argument. You can always refine later.

Lesson 4 of 6
Crafting Your Intro

Intros and conclusions are harder than rhetoric within blocks — because you're going from summarization to creation. Here are three rules that make it easier:

Rule 1: Do your argument prep first. Never start with the intro. Write your status quo, outcome, and impact first. Creative thinking takes longer than analytical work — get the substance done, then sprinkle on the seasoning.

Rule 2: Don't start with the first line — start with the theme. Before writing sentence one, identify what your argument is about in one or two sentences. This is like a thesis statement for your speech. For example: "This legislation does too much and hurts too many — that's why we must negate." Once you have the theme, the first line becomes easier because you know where you're heading.

Rule 3: Identify what interests you most about this topic. We're all different people — we connect with different things. Maybe you found a funny story while researching. Maybe you watched an interview that moved you. Maybe the topic makes you feel patriotic, or angry, or hopeful. Write about the thing that interests you most — that's how you get your best writing, because you're writing about something that actually matters to you.

Tyler's Approach
When Tyler debated against carbon capture technology at national finals, his argument was that we tried it before, it didn't work, and passing would raise energy rates. His intro theme: "This legislation does too much and hurts too many." He didn't start with the first sentence — he identified the theme, then built outward from there.

Lesson 5 of 6
Conclusions

Your conclusion has two jobs:

  1. Tie back to your intro theme. If your intro established a lens for the debate — bring it full circle. Judges remember speeches that feel complete, not ones that just trail off.
  2. Tie back to your stakeholders. Return to the people you discussed throughout your speech. Remind judges who is affected and why this vote matters.

A great conclusion makes the judge think: "That speech had a beginning, a middle, and an end — and it was all about the same thing." That coherence is what earns top ranks, because it means you weren't just listing arguments — you were building a case with a unified story.

Lesson 6 of 6
Finding Your Voice

The most important advice Tyler gives: focus on quantity first, quality second. Put a lot of work out there. Test things. Experiment with style — funny intros, serious intros, conversational intros, personal anecdotes. Don't try to nail it on the first attempt.

Think of debate like a funnel: start broad, then narrow. Early on, you try everything — different tones, different approaches, different rhetorical styles. Over time, with more rounds and more experience, you'll naturally discover what works for you. That's when you refine.

Why This Matters
Not all topics will move you the same way. Some will make you feel patriotic. Some will make you angry. Some will make you laugh. Change up your intros accordingly — and try your best not to recycle them, even on the same topic. When you write about what genuinely interests you, it comes through in your performance. Judges notice authenticity.

Don't overthink it. Don't dig for the perfect line. Just write. Then chisel. Then perform. That's how rhetoric gets built — one draft at a time.
