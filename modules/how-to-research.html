Lesson 1 of 5
Start with the Bill

You've learned how to build arguments and deploy them strategically. Now it's time to learn where those arguments come from — the research process that feeds everything you've practiced.

Before you Google anything, before you open a single article, before you even think about your argument — read the actual legislation. This is the single most common mistake novice debaters make: they research the topic without reading the bill itself.

Why does this matter? Because a bill about "healthcare reform" could do a hundred different things. It could expand Medicaid. It could cap insulin prices. It could create a public option. If you research "healthcare reform" generally, you'll waste hours reading about things the bill doesn't even address.

When you read a bill, ask yourself four questions:

1. What does it DO? What specific action does the bill take? Does it fund a program, ban a practice, create an agency, change a law?

2. What does it FUND? Follow the money. Where does the funding come from? How much? This tells you what the bill actually prioritizes.

3. What does it CHANGE? What's different about the world if this bill passes versus the status quo? This is the foundation of every argument you'll make.

4. What does it NOT do? Equally important — what's missing? If a climate bill funds solar panels but ignores nuclear energy, that's a potential neg argument.

Example: Reading a Minimum Wage Bill
Suppose the bill raises the federal minimum wage to $17/hour over three years. Reading the bill tells you: it phases in (not overnight), it applies federally (not state-by-state), and it includes no exemptions for small businesses. Each of those details becomes an argument — the phase-in addresses "economic shock" concerns, the federal scope creates uniformity, and the lack of small business exemptions is a potential neg angle.

Google Scholar & Source Hierarchy

Not all sources are created equal. In Congressional Debate, the strength of your evidence is directly tied to the credibility of your source. Judges — especially experienced ones — can tell the difference between a debater who cites the Brookings Institution and one who cites a random blog post.

Here's the key test from Ascend: "Would an actual member of Congress use this source?" If the answer is yes, it's strong. If not, dig deeper.

Here's the source hierarchy from strongest to weakest:

Tier 1 (High Quality):

  • Think tanks — Brookings Institution, Carnegie Endowment, Peterson Institute. These provide data-driven policy recommendations to Congress. Use the Harvard Kennedy School think tank search engine to find them.
  • Academic institutions & journals — Harvard, Stanford, Yale + journals like the American Economic Review and Journal of Public Economics. Use Google Scholar to search.
  • Congressional research agencies — The Congressional Research Service (CRS) and Congressional Budget Office (CBO) exist specifically to provide nonpartisan analysis to Congress. Search directly at CRS Reports or CBO.gov.
  • Select news publications — Only the highest quality: the New York Times, Wall Street Journal, Washington Post, LA Times, The Economist, and Bloomberg. These produce original journalism with value-add analysis.

Low Quality (avoid these):

  • Most news outlets — ABC, AP, CBS, CNN, Fox News, HuffPost, NBC, Newsweek, Reuters, Time. These tend to share events rather than produce original analysis.
  • Publicly created sources — Wikipedia, social media, Reddit. Fine for background research, never for citing in round.
  • Opinion pieces & editorials — One person's view, not a vetted finding.
How to Use Google Scholar
Go to scholar.google.com. Type your search terms. Click "Since 2022" on the left sidebar to filter for recent research. Look at the citation count — a paper cited 200+ times is well-established in the field. Click "Cited by" to find newer papers that build on that research. Pro tip: if a paper is behind a paywall, click "All versions" — often a free PDF exists on the author's university page.

Lesson 3 of 5
The Keyword Strategy

Here's a secret that separates good researchers from great ones: don't search the topic — search the MECHANISM.

What does this mean? Most debaters see a bill about universal pre-K and search "universal pre-K" or "early childhood education." They get the same generic articles everyone else finds. The same arguments. The same evidence. Nothing unique.

Great researchers search the mechanism — the specific way the bill works. Instead of "universal pre-K," they search:

"universal pre-K long-term outcomes" — finds studies tracking kids who went through pre-K programs into adulthood.

"pre-K workforce participation mothers" — finds research on how free childcare affects maternal employment rates.

"Head Start program effectiveness meta-analysis" — finds comprehensive reviews of the closest existing program.

Each of these searches targets a specific mechanism: long-term educational outcomes, labor force participation, and program effectiveness. They produce specific, unique evidence that generic searches miss.

Keyword Strategy in Action: Criminal Justice Reform
Bill: Eliminates mandatory minimums for nonviolent drug offenses. Generic search: "criminal justice reform" (gives you everything, helps you with nothing). Mechanism searches: "mandatory minimum recidivism rates" (does sentencing length affect reoffending?), "judicial discretion sentencing outcomes" (what happens when judges can choose?), "drug offense incarceration cost per inmate" (what's the financial burden?). Each mechanism search produces a different card for a different block of your speech.

Lesson 4 of 5
Building a Source Library

Raw research is useless if you can't find it when you need it. The difference between a debater who fumbles for evidence mid-speech and one who delivers it effortlessly is organization.

The best system: organize your evidence into cards sorted by which block they support. Every piece of evidence you find should be tagged with three things:

1. The source: Author/organization, publication, date. Write this exactly as you'd cite it in round: "2024 Brookings Institution study by Dr. Sarah Chen."

2. The block it supports: Is this a SQUO card (proves the problem exists), an Outcome card (proves the mechanism works), or an Impact card (proves why it matters)?

3. The key finding: One sentence summarizing what this source proves. Not a quote — a summary in your own words that you can deliver from memory.

📄
SQUO Card
Source: BLS Employment Report, March 2024. Block: Status Quo. Finding: 38% of minimum wage workers hold two or more jobs to meet basic expenses.
📄
Outcome Card
Source: UC Berkeley Labor Center, 2023. Block: Outcome. Finding: Cities that raised minimum wage to $15 saw a 12% reduction in workers holding multiple jobs within two years.
📄
Impact Card
Source: American Journal of Public Health, 2023. Block: Impact. Finding: Workers holding multiple jobs have 40% higher rates of depression and 25% higher rates of cardiovascular disease.

Lesson 5 of 5
Research Efficiency

The biggest trap in research is the rabbit hole. You start looking for one piece of evidence and suddenly it's 45 minutes later and you've read six fascinating articles that have nothing to do with your argument. Research without a system is just reading.

Here's the system that keeps you efficient:

Set a timer. Give yourself 20 minutes per argument. When the timer goes off, stop. Assess what you have and decide if you need more time or if you should move on.

Use a research checklist. For each argument, you need at minimum:

1 SQUO card (proves the problem exists in the current system)

1 Outcome card (proves the bill's mechanism actually works)

1 Impact angle (shows why the outcome matters to real people)

Once you have all three, you have a complete argument. Then you can refine — find a second SQUO card, a stronger Outcome source, a more specific Impact statistic. But don't refine until you have the basics for every argument you're prepping.

The priority order: Outcome evidence first (hardest to find and most important), then SQUO (usually easy — the problem is well-documented), then Impact (often flows naturally from your Outcome research).

The 20-Minute Research Sprint
Minutes 0-5: Read the bill provision related to your argument. Identify the mechanism. Minutes 5-12: Search for Outcome evidence using mechanism keywords. Find at least one source that proves the mechanism works. Minutes 12-17: Search for SQUO evidence. Find at least one source quantifying the current problem. Minutes 17-20: Write your card summaries. Label each card with source, block, and key finding. Done. You now have one complete argument ready to build into a block.
