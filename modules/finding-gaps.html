Finding Gaps & Generating Arguments
How to identify strengths and weaknesses in a debate, predict gaps, and create arguments that fill them.
Lesson 1 of 5
What Is a Gap?

A gap is an argument or angle that hasn't been covered yet in the round. It's the missing piece of the debate, the thing judges are waiting to hear that no one has said.

Judges reward speakers who add something NEW to the debate. If three speakers have all argued the same economic angle on aff, the judge doesn't need a fourth version of that argument. They want to hear something different, maybe the humanitarian angle, or the constitutional question, or a completely different mechanism within the bill.

Here's why gaps matter strategically: the speaker who fills a gap gets sole ownership of that argument. No one else has made it, so the judge associates it entirely with you. That's how you stand out in a chamber of 15+ speakers, not by saying the same thing louder, but by saying the thing no one else has said.

The Gap Principle
In a round where 12 speakers each make economic arguments, the speaker who makes the one humanitarian argument owns 100% of that lane. In the judge's mind, that speaker contributed something irreplaceable. That's the power of finding gaps.

Lesson 2 of 5
Predicting Gaps During Prep

Gap-finding starts before the round even begins. During prep, your job isn't just to write one good speech; it's to map out the entire argument landscape so you can predict what others will say and find the spaces they'll leave empty.

Here's the process: brainstorm both sides fully. List every argument you can think of for aff and neg. Now ask yourself: which of these are the obvious arguments? The ones that require the least research and sound the most intuitive? Those will be covered first, probably in the first cycle.

The arguments that require deeper research, more creative thinking, or a less obvious connection to the bill? Those are your gaps. Prep the non-obvious argument. While everyone else is writing the easy economic argument, you should be building the block that tackles the constitutional angle, the implementation question, or the second-order effect that nobody else will think of.

Example: Education Funding Bill
"OBVIOUS ARGUMENTS (everyone will make these): 'More funding = better schools' on aff, 'Too expensive' on neg. GAP ARGUMENTS (prep these): 'Funding without accountability measures has historically failed, see Kansas City experiment' on neg, or 'This bill's Title I provision specifically targets rural districts that current formulas skip' on aff. The gap arguments are harder to find but far more valuable in the round."

Lesson 3 of 5
Reading the Room

Prep gets you ready. But the real gap-finding happens live, during the round. The best debaters are constantly tracking what's been said, what hasn't, and where the opening is.

Here's a practical system: keep a running list on your flow. Draw two columns, one for arguments that have been made, one for angles that are still open. Every time a speaker finishes, update both columns. By the time it's your turn to speak, you should know exactly what the judges have heard and what they haven't.

Pay special attention to the quality of what's been covered, not just the topic. If someone made a weak economic argument with no evidence, that angle might still be a gap worth filling, but only if you can do it significantly better. If someone made a strong economic argument, that lane is closed. Move on.

The most common mistake? Debaters write their speech in the first 10 minutes and then stop listening. They deliver a speech that was already made by someone else two speakers ago. Never stop updating your speech based on what's happening in the round.

✅ COVERED (don't repeat)
• Economic growth (Speaker 2, strong)
• Jobs created (Speaker 4, decent)
• Cost concerns (Speaker 3, weak)
🎯 OPEN (your opportunity)
• Public health angle
• Civil rights question
• Implementation specifics
• Historical precedent of failure

Lesson 4 of 5
Generating Arguments from Gaps

Finding a gap is step one. Step two is building a full argument to fill it, often in real time, during the round. This is where the Block Format becomes your best friend.

Once you identify a gap, build a block for it using the structure you already know: SQUO → Outcome → Impact. The Status Quo establishes the problem that no one has talked about yet. The Outcome shows how the bill addresses it. The Impact shows why it matters.

The best late-round speeches are built IN the round, targeting exactly what judges haven't heard yet. Here's a practical approach:

1. Identify the gap, what angle is missing from the debate?

2. Write a one-sentence SQUO, what's the current problem related to this angle? Even without a specific card, you can use common knowledge or logical reasoning.

3. Connect it to the bill's mechanism, how does the bill specifically address this problem? Reference a section if you can.

4. Impact it out, why does this matter for real people? This is where your speech becomes memorable.

Building a Block from a Gap (Immigration Bill)
Gap identified: Everyone has argued economics, no one has discussed family reunification.

Block built in-round:
  • SQUO: The average wait time for family-based immigration visas exceeds 20 years. Families are separated for decades.
  • Outcome: Section 3 of this bill doubles the number of family-based visas issued annually, cutting wait times in half.
  • Impact: For the 4 million families currently waiting, this bill means children will actually grow up knowing their parents.

Lesson 5 of 5
The Gap Mindset

The debaters who consistently rank highest aren't the ones with the most prep. They're not the ones with the thickest evidence binders or the most polished delivery. They're the ones who listen to the round and position their speech to fill the biggest hole.

This is the gap mindset, and it's the ultimate strategic skill in Congress. It requires three things:

1. Active listening. You can't find gaps if you're writing your speech instead of listening. The round IS your prep. Every speech another person gives is information about where the gap is.

2. Flexibility. You have to be willing to abandon your prepped speech if the round demands something different. The debater who clings to their prep even when it's been covered will always lose to the debater who adapts.

3. Confidence in your ability to build on the fly. This is a skill you develop with practice. The first time you build a speech in-round, it will feel uncomfortable. By the tenth time, it will feel like a superpower because you'll be delivering exactly what judges want to hear, every single time.

Championship Pattern
Watch national finals rounds closely. The speakers who finish 1st almost never give the "obvious" speech. They find the angle nobody else thought of, they build it clearly using block format, and they deliver it with conviction. The gap mindset is what separates national finalists from everyone else in the chamber.
