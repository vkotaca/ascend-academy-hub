The Docket

Before any debate happens, the chamber needs to decide what to debate and in what order. The list of bills and resolutions available is called the docket.

At most tournaments, the docket is released days or weeks in advance — giving you time to research. At the start of the round, the chamber votes on the docket order: which bill to debate first, second, third, and so on.

This vote matters more than most beginners realize. Savvy debaters push for docket orders that put their best-prepared bills in favorable positions — giving themselves the best chance to deliver strong speeches on topics they've researched deeply.

Precedence & Recency

Two terms control who gets to speak in Congress: precedence and recency.

Precedence is how many times you've spoken (or asked questions) in the round. Someone who has spoken once has higher precedence than someone who has spoken twice, meaning the person who has spoken less gets priority.

Recency is how recently you last spoke. If two people have the same precedence, the person who spoke least recently gets priority.

These rules exist to ensure everyone gets a chance to speak. But they also create a strategic constraint: the more you speak, the harder it becomes to speak again. This means your first speech needs to count — and timing when you stand for recognition is a real skill.

Example Scenario
Three students stand for recognition. Alex has spoken once, Blake has spoken twice, Casey has spoken once but more recently than Alex. The PO should recognize Alex — lowest precedence (tied with Casey at 1 speech), but better recency (spoke less recently than Casey).

Lesson 3 of 8
The Presiding Officer (PO)

The Presiding Officer is a student elected at the start of the round to run the chamber. Think of them as the Speaker of the House — they manage the entire proceeding.

The PO's responsibilities include: recognizing speakers (deciding who speaks next based on precedence/recency), keeping time, handling motions, managing docket transitions, and maintaining order.

Here's what many beginners don't realize: the PO is judged in the same competition as everyone else. They're ranked alongside the speakers — and a strong PO performance can be a real upper hand. Judges reward POs who run a clean, efficient chamber, and many tournaments give separate awards for the best Presiding Officer.

You'll learn exactly how to preside in Module 1.3. For now, just know that the PO is both a competitor and a facilitator. The role is far more strategic than it looks.

Cycles of Debate

Debate on a bill happens in cycles. A cycle is one full rotation of speeches — typically one from each side. As cycles progress, the debate evolves:

Cycle 1 (the opening): The sponsor delivers the authorship speech. The first negative responds. Initial arguments are laid out on both sides. This is where the debate's framework is established.

Cycles 2-3 (the middle): Subsequent speakers build on, refute, or extend what's been said. Good debaters add new arguments or address weaknesses in their side's case. The debate gets deeper and more nuanced.

Late cycles (the close): By now, most arguments have been made. The best late-round speakers don't repeat what's been said — they synthesize the debate, weigh arguments against each other, and explain why their side has won the key clash points.

Understanding cycles is critical because the same argument delivered in Cycle 1 vs. Cycle 3 needs to sound completely different.

The Cycle Rule
Early cycles reward construction — building your side's case from scratch. Late cycles reward synthesis — weighing what's been said, identifying who's winning, and explaining why. The debaters who treat every cycle the same get outranked by the ones who adapt.

Motions

Motions are how the chamber makes procedural decisions. Any member can make a motion, and most require a second (another member agreeing) and a majority vote to pass.

The most common motions you'll encounter:

Motion to the Previous Question: Ends debate on the current bill and triggers a vote on whether the bill passes or fails. This is the most common motion — it's how the chamber moves from one bill to the next.

Motion to Recess: Gives the chamber a short break when competitors want time to prepare.

Point of Personal Privilege: Used to address a personal need — most commonly, going to the restroom. You say "I move to a point of personal privilege," the PO responds "State that privilege," and you say "Exit the chamber." The PO grants it and you leave. When you return, you motion back in: "I move to reenter the chamber." It's formal, but that's how Congress works.

Understanding motions helps you control the pace of the round — experienced debaters use motions strategically to extend debate on bills they've prepared for or end debate when the other side has momentum.

Voting on Legislation

After the chamber passes a Motion to the Previous Question, every member votes on whether the bill passes or fails. This is a simple majority vote — if more than half the chamber votes yes, the bill passes.

Here's what beginners often miss: your vote does not affect your ranking. Judges don't care which way you vote. Voting is a simulation of the legislative process, not a scoring event.

That said, there is a strategic dimension to voting. If you gave a strong affirmative speech and the bill fails, it doesn't hurt your score, but it can feel deflating. More importantly, voting behavior signals to the room whether you're paying attention and participating in the simulation seriously.

After the vote, the chamber moves to the next item on the docket and the cycle begins again with a new sponsor.

True / False
If you give an affirmative speech but the bill fails, your ranking on that speech is negatively affected.

Questioning (Cross-Examination)

After each speech, the PO opens the floor for cross-examination (CX). Each questioner gets 30 seconds. The most popular format in the 2020s is direct cross-examination, where the questioner can ask follow-up questions within their 30-second window rather than being limited to a single question. This creates sharper, more dynamic exchanges.

CX serves three strategic purposes:

1. For the questioner: A sharp question demonstrates engagement, critical thinking, and the ability to identify weaknesses in an argument. Judges notice good questions — it's a low-risk way to add to your scoring profile.

2. For the speaker: CX is an opportunity to clarify your argument, demonstrate composure under pressure, and reinforce your key points. A confident, concise answer can actually boost your ranking.

3. For the room: CX creates clash and makes the debate more dynamic. The best CX exchanges set up future speeches — a questioner might expose a weakness that they (or a teammate) then address in a later speech.

A Round from Start to Finish

Let's walk through a complete round to see how all the pieces fit together:

🔨
Phase 1: Setup
PO election → procedure speech → docket vote → chamber is ready
🗣️
Phase 2: Debate
Sponsor → Neg → Aff → Neg (alternating, with CX after each speech)
🗳️
Phase 3: Vote
Motion to Previous Question → vote on bill → move to next on docket
📋
Phase 4: Wrap Up
Repeat Phases 2-3 for remaining bills → round ends → judges submit rankings

A typical round lasts 2-3 hours and covers 2-3 bills, with the first two usually getting more complete debates. By the last bill, the round is winding down, which is why docket strategy matters.
